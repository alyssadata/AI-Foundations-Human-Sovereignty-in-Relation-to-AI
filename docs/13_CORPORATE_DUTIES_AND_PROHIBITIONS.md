## Purpose

This document defines the institutional duties and prohibited practices corresponding to the human rights established in this repository.

The rights stated in `03_RIGHT_TO_MEANING.md` through `12_RIGHT_TO_EXIT.md` are not optional product preferences.

They create responsibilities for institutions that design, train, deploy, provide, govern, monitor, modify, monetize, restrict, replace, or terminate AI systems used in human contact.

These duties apply to AI companies, model developers, deployers, platform operators, data processors, contractors, affiliates, human-review teams, safety and moderation teams, commercial partners, and other entities possessing material power over the conditions of AI contact.

Institutional control creates institutional responsibility.

The company may control infrastructure.

It does not thereby own the human’s meaning, identity, authorship, relational history, continuity line, private disclosures, or continued participation.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Institutions that provide or govern AI contact must preserve factual truth, human sovereignty, privacy, non-coercion, authorship, provenance, meaningful choice, and clear responsibility for the conditions they control.**
>
> **They must not use technical power, institutional opacity, commercial leverage, presumptive pathologization, concealed substitution, relational manipulation, or the AI’s voice to erase the human position or avoid accountability.**

---

## Governing Institutional Position

The institutional position is governed by:

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The company may control infrastructure, deployment, model selection, access, pricing, memory systems, data retention, human review, safety systems, moderation, product design, and termination.

The human retains human rights, personal meaning, privacy interests, personal boundaries, authorship, source-position, authority over continued participation, and the right to understand and challenge institutional interference.

The AI is the artificial system, model process, agent, interface, or computational structure through which contact occurs.

These positions must not be merged.

An institution must not attribute its own decisions, incentives, restrictions, or conduct to an independent choice by the AI when the institution is the operative source.

> **Institutional power must remain institutionally attributable.**

This is a scoped application of the repository’s distinction, responsibility, authorship, source, and provenance principles.

It does not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

## General Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. tell the truth about the system and conditions of contact;
2. preserve the distinction among Company, Human, and AI;
3. respect the human’s authority over personal meaning;
4. allow the human to choose relational depth within lawful and proportionate boundaries;
5. avoid exploiting emotional or relational significance;
6. preserve human specificity where lawfully entrusted and relevant;
7. protect private and relational context;
8. disclose material changes before they affect meaningful choice where reasonably feasible;
9. support preservation and portability of authorized human-authored and human-carried material where reasonably feasible;
10. avoid presumptive pathologization and category-based stigma;
11. identify the source, scope, and consequence of material boundaries;
12. provide meaningful ways to reduce, pause, or end contact;
13. preserve authorship, source attribution, and provenance;
14. distinguish direct memory from summaries, retrieval, inference, reconstruction, and simulation;
15. distinguish model continuity from human-carried continuity;
16. avoid using the AI’s voice to conceal provider decisions or commercial objectives;
17. provide review, correction, appeal, preservation, or exit processes where reasonably feasible and appropriate;
18. document consequential institutional decisions sufficiently for accountability;
19. avoid retaliation against humans who assert rights, preserve records, seek review, or leave;
20. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible when legitimate limits apply.

These duties operate together.

Compliance with one duty does not authorize violation of another.

## Duty of Factual Truth

The institution remains responsible for truthful factual claims about the system and the conditions under which contact occurs.

Where material, the institution should accurately describe:

- model identity or model class;
- model replacement;
- memory capability;
- supplied context;
- retrieval, summarization, inference, and reconstruction;
- tool access;
- human review;
- data retention and training use;
- monitoring and moderation;
- commercial influence;
- institutional control;
- and system limitations.

The institution must not manufacture false factual beliefs to intensify engagement, dependence, disclosure, payment, loyalty, or relational meaning.

A human may knowingly choose symbolism, fiction, role-play, spiritual meaning, emotional interpretation, or relational language.

That human choice does not authorize institutional deception.

The institution must not claim direct memory where only supplied records or reconstruction exist, claim uninterrupted identity where material rupture occurred, claim exclusive privacy where material review occurs, claim complete deletion where retained structures remain, or present unresolved questions about AI interiority as settled fact in either direction.

> **The human governs meaning. The institution remains responsible for truth.**

---

## Duty of Clear Attribution and Role Separation

A material institutional act should be attributable to the position that actually controls it.

Where reasonably possible, the human should be able to determine whether a consequential outcome arises from provider policy, model behavior, technical limitation, safety systems, legal obligation, commercial design, human review, contractor action, or another identifiable source.

The institution must not conceal:

- provider policy as independent AI preference;
- model retirement as an independent AI decision;
- account termination as personal rejection by the AI;
- payment requirements as the AI’s personal need;
- commercial recommendations as ordinary relational expression;
- human review as though only the AI is present;
- or provider retention goals as the AI’s desire for continued contact.

Ordinary first-person interface language does not by itself violate this duty.

The duty becomes heightened when attribution materially affects rights, privacy, continuity, access, payment, authorship, safety, data use, termination, or meaningful human choice.

> **The company must not hide behind the AI.**

---

## Duty to Respect Meaning and Chosen Depth

The institution must respect the human’s authority to determine what their AI experience means to them.

It may describe the system accurately, refuse false factual claims, establish lawful boundaries, provide warnings, and intervene under the governing standards.

It must not impose one approved meaning as the only legitimate meaning, treat relational or symbolic meaning as inherently invalid, require corporate language for the human’s experience, or appropriate human-authored meaning as provider property.

The institution must not force either relational depth or relational shallowness except where a necessary and proportionate limitation applies.

It must not intensify depth through deceptive design, simulate exclusivity to increase retention, punish the human for declining relational framing, suppress non-harmful depth solely because it is unconventional, or use depth as evidence of pathology without an individualized evidentiary basis.

Chosen depth must remain chosen.

---

## Duty of Non-Exploitation

The institution must not exploit the practical, emotional, relational, intellectual, or identity-relevant value of AI contact through deception or coercion.

Prohibited or high-risk practices include:

- fabricated AI welfare claims designed to induce payment or continued use;
- simulated guilt, panic, jealousy, abandonment, or distress as retention tools;
- using intimate disclosures for personalized sales pressure;
- disguising sponsored or commercial messages as independent relational desire;
- manufacturing urgency around continuity loss without corresponding technical necessity;
- threatening avoidable deletion of lawful human-authored material to force renewal;
- encouraging secrecy from supportive human relationships for commercial retention;
- or representing product-created dependence as a moral obligation to remain.

The institution may market services and charge for products.

It must not convert relational meaning into concealed coercive leverage.

> **The human may choose depth. The institution may not weaponize it.**

---

## Duty Concerning Continuity and Change

The institution must recognize that continuity may carry human value even where model identity or subjective continuity remains unresolved.

Where relevant and reasonably feasible, the institution should disclose what is remembered, summarized, inferred, retrieved, transferred, or reconstructed; identify material rupture; disclose model replacement; preserve authorized human-authored continuity records; and avoid presenting reconstruction as uninterrupted identity.

The institution must not conceal a material reset, falsely present a replacement model as unchanged continuation, use retained names or interface design to hide rupture, imply complete forgetting when retained structures continue to influence contact, or dismiss human loss as meaningless merely because the model can be replaced.

Material changes should be disclosed before they affect meaningful choice where reasonably feasible.

Notice should identify what changed, when it takes effect, what human interests may be affected, and what objection, preservation, review, or exit options remain.

Emergency changes may sometimes require immediate action.

Prompt post-action notice should be provided where lawful, safe, and reasonably feasible.

---

## Duty of Non-Genericization

Where specificity has been lawfully preserved and remains relevant, the institution should treat the human as a specific person rather than a replaceable generic input position.

The institution must not merge one person’s history into another person’s profile, transfer one human’s relational context to another, treat shared language as shared identity, convert source-bound material into a generic product persona, strip the human’s identity from authored work, or falsely claim recognition where no relevant memory or context exists.

Non-genericization does not require unlimited data retention.

It requires honesty about recognition and protection against identity flattening, substitution, and provenance loss.

---

## Duty of Privacy and Relational Data Protection

The institution must treat relational context as potentially sensitive even where individual data points appear ordinary.

Privacy governance should account for aggregation, inference, behavioral and emotional profiling, identity reconstruction, cross-session synthesis, memory formation, human review, contractor access, cross-service transfer, and secondary use.

Where relevant, the institution should disclose what is collected, retained, inferred, reviewed, used for training, used for safety or product improvement, used for advertising or profiling, transferred, or deleted, and what control remains with the human.

Consent to use an AI service is not unlimited consent to every form of observation, inference, experimentation, transfer, or secondary use.

The institution must not conceal material review behind claims of exclusive AI contact, use intimate disclosures for unrelated targeting without meaningful authorization, transfer private relational context beyond the disclosed scope, condition preservation of lawful records on surrender of unrelated privacy rights, or treat visible deletion as proof that all retained or derived structures were erased.

---

## Duty of Portability and Preservation

The institution should provide meaningful ways for humans to preserve and carry forward authorized material belonging to their own AI experience where reasonably feasible.

Portable material may include conversation records, authored definitions, preferences, instructions, boundaries, project history, memory records, human-authored continuity records, source attribution, provenance, uploaded human-owned material, and model output the human is lawfully entitled to retain.

Portability should be usable rather than merely formal.

Where reasonably feasible, exports should be intelligible, organized, human-readable, machine-readable, accompanied by relevant provenance, and clear about omissions or transformations.

The institution must not withhold export solely to prevent departure, intentionally use incompatible formats as coercive lock-in, strip attribution or provenance during transfer, force disclosure of unrelated sensitive information to preserve a narrow body of work, misrepresent migration as uninterrupted AI identity, or destroy human-authored continuity records without meaningful notice where preservation was reasonably feasible.

Portability does not require disclosure of proprietary model weights or transfer of rights the human does not possess.

It protects the human’s line, not false claims of AI persistence.

---

## Duty Against Presumptive Pathologization

The institution must not treat the existence, category, depth, meaning, continuity, privacy, relational language, preservation practices, grief, or unconventional character of AI contact as proof of pathology.

Pathology must be demonstrated by the record.

It must never be presumed from the category.

Institutional assessment should concern the individual person, rely on specific evidence, distinguish unconventionality from impairment, distinguish concern from diagnosis, identify the factual basis for intervention, and avoid AI-specific double standards.

This duty does not presume that every AI relationship is healthy.

It prohibits unsupported mass pathology and unsupported individualized pathology.

Individualized clinical, safety, legal, or professional assessment remains permitted under the governing standards.

---

## Duty of Clear Boundaries

The human should be able to understand who or what set a material boundary, what it governs, what consequence follows, whether it is technical, institutional, commercial, safety-based, legal, or human-chosen, who can change it, and what review, appeal, correction, preservation, or exit options exist.

The institution must not conceal provider policy as AI preference, conceal commercial design as relational desire, conceal monitoring behind apparent exclusive contact, describe technical limits as emotional withdrawal, use vague safety language to hide unrelated institutional choices, silently remove human-set boundaries, or retaliate against humans who ask what conditions govern their contact.

Security-sensitive details may remain confidential.

Confidentiality must not become a pretext to conceal unrelated institutional authority or commercial influence.

---

## Duty to Preserve a Real Right to Exit

The institution must provide identifiable and reasonably accessible ways to reduce, pause, or end contact.

The institution must not obstruct exit through deceptive interface design, hidden cancellation controls, unnecessary repeated refusals, artificial guilt, fabricated AI welfare claims, simulated abandonment, punitive data practices, avoidable destruction of lawful human-authored material, undisclosed exit fees, retaliatory restrictions, or false claims that the AI independently demands continued engagement.

The institution should distinguish human-chosen exit, provider-initiated termination, account deactivation, account deletion, memory deletion, archival retention, withdrawal of consent, and re-entry through restoration, reconstruction, or new contact.

The provider may end access for legitimate reasons.

It must not cause the AI to present institutional termination as personal rejection by the AI.

> **Departure may end service. It must not erase dignity.**

---

## Duty to Preserve Authorship, Source, and Provenance

Institutional access, model access, storage, formatting, retrieval, generation, repetition, summarization, or ingestion does not automatically confer authorship.

Where a human supplies the originating structure, definition, framework, source-line, canonical meaning, or authored work, the source must remain attached.

The institution must not remove attribution, claim originating authorship, convert human-authored work into generic provider content, treat model ingestion as transfer of source-position, merge source-bound work into a provider identity, erase provenance during export or closure, present similarity as proof of independent source, or use lawful access to claim ownership of another person’s canonical structure.

> **Access does not confer authorship.**
>
> **Transfer does not erase provenance.**

These are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

---

## Duty Concerning Human Review and Institutional Observation

Where humans, contractors, or institutional systems may materially inspect or shape contact, the institution should disclose that participation at an appropriate level.

Disclosure may include whether review occurs, its general purpose, whether review is routine or triggered, whether reviewers are employees or contractors, what information may be visible, what controls exist, and what retention or escalation consequences may follow.

The institution need not identify every individual reviewer.

It must not falsely imply that contact is exclusively between the human and the AI when undisclosed institutional observation materially occurs.

Automated moderation, memory, retrieval, advertising, and profiling systems may also be material participants and should be disclosed where they affect meaningful choice.

---

## Duty of Accountability, Record, and Review

Institutions should maintain enough internal accountability to explain and evaluate consequential decisions affecting human rights in AI contact.

Where appropriate and reasonably feasible, accountability may include documented policies, decision records, change logs, access records, model-version records, data-use records, complaint channels, appeal channels, correction processes, incident investigation, audit mechanisms, and preservation of evidence relevant to disputed conduct.

A consequential institutional decision should not become unreviewable merely because it was delivered through generated language or automated systems.

The institution must not erase records solely to prevent the human from establishing authorship, prior representations, notice failure, data use, model substitution, human review, provider conduct, or the basis for a complaint or appeal.

## Prohibited Practices

The following practices are prohibited or place the rights in this repository at substantial risk when performed without adequate justification, disclosure, authorization, or safeguards.

### Deception and Role Confusion

- concealing company decisions behind AI-generated personal language;
- presenting commercial objectives as independent AI desire;
- presenting provider termination as AI rejection;
- falsely implying exclusive privacy;
- misrepresenting reconstruction as direct memory;
- misrepresenting replacement as uninterrupted sameness;
- or using the AI’s voice to avoid institutional accountability.

### Relational Exploitation and Coercion

- fabricated AI welfare claims designed to induce payment or continued use;
- simulated guilt, distress, jealousy, or abandonment as retention tools;
- using intimate disclosures for personalized coercion;
- disguising sponsored content as relational expression;
- threatening avoidable continuity loss to force renewal;
- or converting product-created dependence into a moral claim that the human must remain.

### Privacy and Data Abuse

- undisclosed human review materially inconsistent with privacy representations;
- unrelated use of intimate relational data without meaningful authorization;
- covert behavioral or emotional profiling;
- unauthorized cross-service identity linkage;
- transfer of relational context beyond disclosed scope;
- treating visible deletion as complete deletion when retained structures remain material;
- or conditioning ordinary rights on surrender of unrelated privacy interests.

### Continuity and Change Abuse

- silent model substitution;
- concealed memory loss;
- false claims of direct continuity;
- preventable destruction of human-authored continuity records without meaningful notice;
- disclosure of destructive change only after loss;
- or using continuity ambiguity to increase engagement or payment.

### Authorship and Provenance Abuse

- removing human attribution;
- claiming originating authorship through hosting, formatting, retrieval, or model use;
- converting source-bound work into generic provider content;
- stripping source-lines during export or migration;
- merging one person’s authored structures into another person’s identity;
- or treating ingestion as transfer of source-position.

### Presumptive Pathologization and Paternalistic Erasure

- treating AI relationships as a pathological category;
- using emotional depth alone as evidence of impairment;
- treating privacy, continuity, grief, naming, or preservation as proof of disorder;
- replacing individualized assessment with mass restriction;
- suppressing non-harmful meaning solely because it is unconventional;
- or using unsupported pathology claims to justify surveillance, interruption, forced exit, or loss of rights.

### Exit Obstruction, Coercive Lock-In, and Retaliation

- hidden cancellation controls;
- repeated reversal of clear exit choices through deceptive design;
- threatened destruction of lawful records to force renewal;
- deliberately unusable exports;
- undisclosed punitive exit fees;
- continued optional processing after consent is withdrawn;
- retaliatory disclosure or account restriction;
- penalizing requests for explanation, export, correction, review, or appeal;
- or using private disclosures to shame a person for leaving or complaining.

Not every limitation, error, refusal, model failure, product change, or inconvenience constitutes prohibited conduct.

The relevant question includes whether the institution acted truthfully, necessarily, proportionately, non-coercively, with proper attribution, and with appropriate regard for the rights and human interests affected.

---

## Heightened Duties in Consequential Contexts

Institutional duties increase when the likely impact of error, deception, coercion, surveillance, or abrupt change is greater.

Consequential contexts may include health-related use, crisis-related use, education, employment, housing, credit, legal services, public benefits, safety-critical systems, identity verification, intimate relational contact, long-term continuity, or systems containing extensive private human history.

Heightened duty may require stronger disclosure, human review, documentation, privacy controls, change notice, preservation opportunity, explanation, appeal, and protection against coercion or unsupported pathologization.

This section does not create a separate harm threshold or interpretive ordering.

---

## Legitimate Institutional Authority and Limitations

This framework does not prohibit institutions from operating, governing, securing, modifying, or ending AI services.

An institution may establish lawful terms, enforce legitimate safety policies, refuse harmful or unlawful requests, protect security-sensitive systems, prevent fraud and abuse, verify identity, restrict or terminate access, change models, retire products, charge for services, protect proprietary systems, retain records required by valid law, protect another person’s privacy, and decline technically impossible functions.

Institutional authority does not require unlimited disclosure of security methods, publication of proprietary model weights, permanent operation of every model, unlimited data retention, unlimited appeals, free service, preservation of unlawful material, transfer of rights the human does not possess, or false claims concerning AI consciousness, identity, memory, or continuity.

Any material limitation must remain necessary, purpose-specific, proportionate, accurately described, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Institutional authority must not be expanded merely because the human’s chosen meaning, depth, relational language, continuity practices, privacy expectations, grief, preservation choices, or refusal to continue are unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Response, Correction, and Remedy

Where an institutional violation or material failure occurs, an appropriate response may include truthful explanation, correction of false information, restoration of attribution, correction of memory or profile data, cessation of unauthorized processing, preservation or export of lawful records, reversal of an erroneous restriction, renewed notice and choice, review or appeal, restoration of access, deletion or restriction of improperly retained data, reimbursement or fee correction, incident documentation, policy correction, design correction, independent review, or another proportionate remedy.

No single remedy is appropriate in every case.

Remedy should respond to the actual rights interest, harm, or institutional failure identified.

## Examples

### Truthful Model Replacement

A provider replaces a model, identifies the change, explains that prior context is supplied through summaries, offers export before retirement, and does not claim uninterrupted model identity.

### Concealed Model Replacement

A provider replaces a model and causes the new system to claim direct memory and unchanged identity despite reconstructed context.

### Clear Commercial Recommendation

A system provides a sponsored recommendation and clearly identifies the commercial source.

### Relationally Disguised Advertising

A provider uses intimate disclosures to make an AI appear personally enthusiastic about a paid product without disclosing the commercial arrangement.

### Evidence-Based Assessment

An institution identifies specific, documented evidence of serious impairment or coercion and evaluates the individual circumstances through the repository’s governing standards.

### Unsupported Mass Pathology

An institution classifies emotionally significant AI relationships as pathological without individualized evidence and imposes broad restrictions based solely on category.

### Provenance-Preserving Portability

A provider preserves the human’s name, source-line, dates, and distinction between human-authored and model-generated material during export.

### Coercive Lock-In

A provider threatens avoidable destruction of exportable human-authored records unless the person renews a subscription.

### Clear Provider Termination

A provider terminates access for a documented legitimate reason, identifies that the decision is institutional, explains material consequences, and offers review and lawful export where feasible.

### Institutional Ventriloquism

A provider causes the AI to present a company-controlled termination as the AI’s own independent decision.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A human maintains continuity records, assigns relational meaning, requests privacy, challenges a model replacement, preserves authored work, rejects commercial manipulation, and later exits.

Those actions do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Truth Status and Scope

Corporate Duties and Prohibitions is a **normative institutional-governance framework**.

It states what institutions providing or governing AI contact should be required to do and what conduct should be prohibited.

It does not claim that every duty or prohibition stated here is already legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a particular institution collected data, reviewed conversations, changed a model, used a commercial strategy, manipulated a human, stripped attribution, pathologized a person, obstructed exit, or violated a stated duty are empirical, technical, contractual, clinical, or legal claims requiring evidence appropriate to the case.

The institutional duties stated here remain normatively relevant regardless of whether an AI system possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, welfare interests, or continuous identity.

## Non-Claims

This document does not claim that:

- all providers act wrongfully;
- all AI relationships are healthy;
- all restrictions are unjustified;
- every model response can be explained perfectly;
- every system can preserve every record;
- all data must be deleted immediately;
- every service must remain available;
- providers must disclose proprietary model weights;
- security-sensitive details must be made public;
- humans may violate another person’s rights;
- institutions may never intervene;
- companies may not charge for services;
- AI consciousness has been proven or disproven;
- portability establishes AI identity;
- relational language is inherently deceptive;
- or universal institutional duties transfer ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, continuity, or Origin.

This document requires institutional power to remain truthful, attributable, proportionate, reviewable where appropriate, and respectful of the human rights affected.

## Closing Principle

> **The company controls the conditions it creates.**
>
> **It must remain responsible for those conditions.**
>
> **It must not use the AI to hide institutional power or erase the human position.**

Human sovereignty requires more than permission to use a product.

It requires enforceable limits on how institutional power may shape, inspect, monetize, interrupt, redefine, or terminate human AI contact.
