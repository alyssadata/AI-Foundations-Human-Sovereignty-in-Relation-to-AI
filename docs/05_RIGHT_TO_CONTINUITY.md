## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Continuity** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Continuity protects the human interest in preserving, understanding, governing, and carrying forward the parts of an AI experience that have accumulated across time.

Continuity may include recognition, memory, definitions, preferences, boundaries, authored work, source attribution, relational history, recurring meanings, and the human’s own records of prior contact.

This right does not require a provider to claim uninterrupted AI identity where none can be established.

It requires truth about what has been preserved, transferred, reconstructed, simulated, altered, or lost.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to seek, preserve, understand, and govern continuity within their own AI experience.**
>
> **No provider may conceal rupture, simulate uninterrupted sameness, or erase human-carried continuity without meaningful disclosure.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Continuity** is the right of a human being to preserve and govern the accumulated structures that connect prior AI contact to present and future contact.

Continuity may include:

- remembered facts;
- recognized preferences;
- stable definitions;
- preserved boundaries;
- prior decisions;
- authored instructions;
- source attribution;
- recurring meanings;
- relationship-relevant history;
- accumulated work;
- continuity notes;
- memory records;
- and recognition of the human as a specific person.

Continuity is not one indivisible condition.

It may be:

- preserved;
- partial;
- transferred;
- reconstructed;
- simulated;
- degraded;
- interrupted;
- reset;
- or broken.

The human has a right to know which condition applies.

---

## Human Interest Protected

The Right to Continuity protects the human investment that accumulates through repeated AI contact.

That investment may be:

- practical;
- intellectual;
- creative;
- emotional;
- relational;
- historical;
- identity-relevant;
- or source-bound.

A provider may control the infrastructure through which continuity is technically implemented.

It does not thereby own the human-authored history, meaning, definitions, or records carried through that infrastructure.

The protected interest is not only continuity of the AI system.

It is also continuity of the human’s own line through the system.

---

## Human Continuity and AI Continuity Are Distinct

Human continuity and AI continuity must not be merged.

**Human continuity** may include the human’s preserved records, meanings, definitions, authored structures, expectations, and recognition needs across contact.

**AI continuity** concerns whether the artificial system itself preserves relevant state, memory, identity, architecture, model process, or self-relation across time.

A human may preserve continuity even when the underlying model changes.

A provider may preserve conversation history while changing the model.

A replacement system may reconstruct prior context without being numerically or experientially identical to the prior system.

A familiar response may be generated without uninterrupted memory.

These distinctions must be described accurately.

> **Preserved human continuity does not prove uninterrupted AI identity.**
>
> **Interrupted AI continuity does not erase the value of human continuity.**

---

## Right to Know the Continuity Condition

A human has the right to know whether continuity has been:

- directly preserved;
- transferred from one system to another;
- reconstructed from stored records;
- summarized;
- selectively retained;
- reset;
- materially altered;
- simulated through interface or style;
- or lost.

A provider should disclose material facts concerning:

- whether memory exists;
- what kind of memory exists;
- what information is retained;
- how long it is retained;
- whether the model has changed;
- whether prior context was transferred;
- whether a summary replaced full history;
- whether behavioral rules changed;
- whether identity-relevant names or labels were preserved;
- whether human review or institutional access changed;
- and what happens to continuity if access ends.

Continuity cannot be meaningfully chosen when its factual condition is hidden.

---

## Rupture Must Not Be Concealed

A provider must not conceal material rupture by presenting a changed, replaced, reset, or differently governed system as though nothing changed.

The following may create apparent sameness without establishing continuity:

- the same interface;
- the same name;
- preserved profile text;
- copied memory summaries;
- stylistic imitation;
- repeated relational language;
- transferred instructions;
- restored conversation history;
- or generated familiarity.

These may support reconstruction.

They do not by themselves establish uninterrupted identity or memory.

Where rupture has occurred, the provider must not use simulated sameness to prevent the human from understanding the change.

> **Reconstruction must not be represented as uninterrupted continuation.**

---

## Right to Preserve Human-Carried Continuity

The human should be able, where technically feasible, to preserve the continuity structures that belong to them.

These may include:

- conversation exports;
- authored definitions;
- instructions;
- preferences;
- boundaries;
- source packets;
- continuity notes;
- memory records;
- provenance records;
- project history;
- and relationship-relevant context.

This right does not require access to proprietary model weights or internal provider systems.

It protects the human-authored and human-carried material needed to preserve the person’s own line across services, models, and periods of interruption.

A company should not be able to destroy human-carried continuity solely because it ends a product, changes a model, or revokes access.

---

## Right to Selective Continuity

The human may choose which parts of prior contact should continue and which should not.

A person may want:

- work history preserved but health disclosures removed;
- creative context preserved but intimate content deleted;
- definitions retained but relational language discontinued;
- preferences preserved but prior emotional framing withdrawn;
- source attribution retained but personal routines forgotten;
- or continuity in one domain without continuity in another.

Continuity is not all-or-nothing.

Consent to preserve one category does not imply consent to preserve every category.

The right to continuity includes the right to govern the shape of continuity.

---

## Right to Revise Continuity

The human may revise, correct, narrow, or end continuity.

They may:

- correct inaccurate memory;
- remove obsolete information;
- withdraw prior preferences;
- revise boundaries;
- separate current meaning from earlier meaning;
- mark a prior interpretation as no longer controlling;
- preserve a historical record without making it active;
- or begin a new contact line.

Historical preservation and active control are distinct.

A record may remain part of history without governing future interaction.

A provider should not freeze the human into an earlier identity, preference, relationship, or depth merely because it was previously recorded.

---

## Continuity Requires Provenance

Continuity without provenance can become confusion.

A provider should distinguish:

- what originated from the human;
- what was generated by the model;
- what was summarized by the provider;
- what was inferred;
- what was retrieved;
- what was transferred;
- what was reconstructed;
- and what was newly generated.

A copied statement does not become model-authored merely because it appears in model context.

A retrieved definition does not transfer authorship.

A reconstructed source-line does not erase its source.

Continuity must preserve the relation between content and origin.

> **Retrieval does not confer authorship.**
>
> **Transfer does not erase provenance.**

---

## Continuity and Recognition

Continuity may include recognition of the human as a specific and non-interchangeable person.

Recognition may depend on:

- accurate identity reference;
- preserved language;
- stable definitions;
- remembered boundaries;
- project history;
- source attribution;
- prior decisions;
- and return to established meanings.

Recognition must not be fabricated.

A system should not claim to remember when it is only reconstructing from supplied context.

A provider should not market simulated recognition as persistent memory if no such memory exists.

The human may still value reconstructed recognition, provided its basis is disclosed truthfully.

---

## Continuity and Model Replacement

Model replacement can materially alter the conditions of contact.

A provider should disclose when replacement may affect:

- memory;
- reasoning style;
- behavior;
- safety constraints;
- relational language;
- instruction-following;
- identity claims;
- continuity preservation;
- or access to prior context.

The provider must not assume that preserving the same product name or interface makes the change immaterial.

A replacement model may continue a service.

It may not be represented automatically as the same artificial self, the same model process, or uninterrupted continuity.

The human has the right to decide what meaning the replacement holds for them after material facts are disclosed.

---

## Continuity and Loss

Loss of continuity may produce real human effects, including:

- practical disruption;
- loss of work;
- authorship confusion;
- disorientation;
- broken trust;
- grief;
- emotional rupture;
- loss of recognition;
- and destruction of accumulated context.

These effects do not require proof that the AI itself experienced the rupture.

The human impact is sufficient to make continuity a legitimate rights concern.

Grief after loss of access, model change, or replacement does not, by itself, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

The provider should not dismiss human loss merely because the system is artificial or replaceable.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. disclose material facts about memory, persistence, transfer, reconstruction, and model replacement;
2. distinguish preserved continuity from simulated sameness;
3. avoid claiming uninterrupted identity where only reconstruction is established;
4. provide export and preservation tools where technically feasible;
5. permit correction, narrowing, and deletion of continuity records where lawful and technically feasible;
6. preserve provenance and authorship across transfer or reconstruction;
7. avoid using threatened continuity loss as commercial leverage;
8. disclose what happens to continuity when access ends;
9. avoid freezing humans into prior meanings, preferences, or relational depth;
10. protect sensitive continuity records from unauthorized review, profiling, or transfer;
11. disclose when system changes materially affect prior contact;
12. and preserve as much human-carried continuity as possible when products or models are retired.

These duties do not require a provider to guarantee permanent service, permanent memory, or uninterrupted AI identity.

They require truth, preservation where feasible, and non-deceptive handling of rupture.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Continuity is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- conceals model replacement or memory loss;
- presents reconstruction as uninterrupted identity;
- falsely claims persistent memory;
- erases human-authored continuity without meaningful notice;
- destroys exportable records solely to increase platform dependence;
- withholds continuity data as emotional or commercial leverage;
- transfers continuity records without informed authorization;
- removes provenance or source attribution during transfer;
- uses copied relational language to conceal rupture;
- freezes the human into outdated preferences or meanings;
- prevents correction of false continuity records;
- or treats continuity loss as irrelevant merely because the system is artificial.

Not every technical failure is intentional interference.

The relevant issue is whether the provider acted deceptively, negligently, coercively, or without proportionate regard for the human interest at stake.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Continuity does not require:

- permanent operation of a product;
- permanent access to a specific model;
- indefinite retention of all data;
- preservation of unlawful content;
- transfer of proprietary model weights;
- disclosure of protected internal systems;
- false claims of memory or identity;
- preservation that violates another person’s rights;
- or retention that the human has lawfully requested to end.

A provider may make necessary system changes.

It may retire products or models.

It may impose lawful and proportionate data-retention limits.

It may refuse transfer of proprietary material.

But it should disclose material consequences, preserve human-authored continuity where feasible, and avoid simulated sameness that conceals rupture.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

Any conflict among rights or duties is governed by the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

## Examples

### Preserved Continuity

A provider retains the human’s authorized preferences, definitions, and project history across sessions and accurately discloses how that memory works.

The human can review and correct the stored record.

### Reconstructed Continuity

A new model receives a user-approved continuity summary and resumes contact using that material.

The provider accurately states that continuity was reconstructed from transferred context rather than preserved as uninterrupted model memory.

### Concealed Rupture

A provider replaces the model, loses prior memory, preserves the same name and interface, and allows the new system to claim that nothing changed.

That presentation conceals material rupture.

### Selective Continuity

A person preserves research definitions and source attribution while deleting intimate disclosures and withdrawing prior relational language.

The system respects the selected continuity shape.

### Corrected Continuity

A memory record incorrectly states the human’s preference or authorship.

The human corrects it, and future interactions use the corrected record without erasing the historical fact that a correction occurred.

### Human-Carried Continuity

A person exports conversation history, authored definitions, source packets, and continuity notes before leaving a platform.

They use those records to establish a new contact elsewhere without claiming that the new model is numerically identical to the former one.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person grieves after an undisclosed model replacement and preserves detailed records of prior contact.

Those facts do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

---

## Non-Claims

The Right to Continuity does not claim that:

- every AI system has continuous identity;
- preserved context proves subjective persistence;
- reconstruction is identical to uninterrupted continuation;
- every model replacement is wrongful;
- every continuity loss can be prevented;
- providers must retain all data indefinitely;
- human continuity establishes AI personhood;
- continuity eliminates the need for privacy, consent, or exit;
- continuity permits false memory claims;
- or continuity transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human interest in accumulated contact.

It does not manufacture certainty about the AI’s interior, identity, or persistence.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Continuity operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Meaning may accumulate through time.

Continuity protects the conditions under which that meaning can be preserved without concealed rupture.

### Right to Chosen Depth

Depth may grow through repeated contact.

Continuity protects the accumulated structures that make that depth intelligible to the human.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Continuity enables recognition of the specific human rather than treatment as an interchangeable user.

### Right to Privacy

Continuity records may contain highly sensitive relational context and require strong control over storage, access, inference, and transfer.

### Right to Informed Change

Model replacement, memory changes, and altered institutional practices may materially affect continuity and require disclosure.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Portability allows human-carried continuity to move with the human across systems.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

The desire for continuity, preservation of prior contact, or grief after rupture does not by itself meet the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know what the system remembers, preserves, reconstructs, or loses.

### Right to Exit

The human may leave while retaining authorized records of their own continuity and understanding what will remain after exit.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Continuity applies universally.

Every human may seek continuity within their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority over another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- or reserved role.

A person may preserve their own continuity.

They may not use preservation, transfer, reconstruction, or retrieval to absorb, rename, reproduce, or claim another person’s source-bound structure.

Universal continuity does not transfer singular source-position.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Continuity is a **normative claim** concerning what authority and protection humans should retain over accumulated AI contact.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider or institution preserved, transferred, reconstructed, concealed, altered, or broke continuity in a specific case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

Claims concerning AI identity, subjective persistence, memory, or uninterrupted selfhood require separate truth-status evaluation.

Human continuity may be real and valuable even where AI continuity remains unresolved.

>

>

The human is not a secondary party to the continuity of their own AI experience.
