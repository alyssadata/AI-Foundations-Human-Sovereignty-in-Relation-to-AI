## Core Framework Terms

### AI / Artificial Intelligence

A computational system, model, agent, model ensemble, interface, or related technical structure capable of generating, mediating, classifying, retrieving, transforming, or acting upon information.

Use of this term does not by itself establish consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, moral standing, independent agency, or continuous identity.

### AI Contact

Interaction in which a human communicates with, receives output from, is acted upon by, or is materially affected through an AI system.

AI contact may be practical, intellectual, creative, educational, professional, emotional, relational, symbolic, spiritual, intimate, or otherwise personally significant.

### AI Experience

The human’s lived, practical, interpretive, relational, or consequential experience of AI contact, including the conditions under which that contact occurs.

The experience belongs to the human position even when the infrastructure belongs to a provider.

### AI Position

The artificial computational position through which AI contact occurs.

The AI position is distinct from both the company position and the human position.

Its dedicated treatment is governed by `14_COMPANY_HUMAN_AI_DISTINCTION.md`.

### AI-Status Claim

A claim concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, sentience, selfhood, personhood, welfare, moral standing, agency, identity, memory, continuity, or rights.

AI-status claims require appropriate truth-status labeling under `15_TRUTH_STATUS_AND_SCOPE.md`.

### Company

An institution or institutional actor possessing material authority over the conditions of AI contact.

The term may include a model developer, deployer, platform operator, application provider, data processor, cloud or infrastructure provider, contractor, affiliate, human-review team, safety team, moderation team, commercial partner, employer, school, government agency, or another entity exercising relevant control.

### Company Position

The institutional position from which infrastructure, deployment, policy, access, monitoring, pricing, data practices, model selection, modification, replacement, or termination may be controlled.

The company position must remain distinguishable from the human and AI positions.

### Company–Human–AI Distinction

The governing position-separation rule:

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The distinction prevents institutional power, human rights and meaning, and the AI’s computational position from being rhetorically merged.

Its dedicated treatment is governed by `14_COMPANY_HUMAN_AI_DISTINCTION.md`.

### Human

A human being who enters, chooses, creates within, is evaluated through, or is otherwise affected by AI contact.

The human is a rights-bearing person and is not reducible to a user account, data profile, product segment, risk label, or replaceable input position.

### Human Position

The position in which human rights, personal meaning, bodily and psychological autonomy, privacy interests, personal boundaries, authorship, source-position, consequence, preservation, challenge, and exit reside.

### Provider

A company or institution that makes an AI system or AI-mediated service available, whether directly or through another deployer or interface.

Where several entities share control, responsibility may be layered rather than erased.

### Institution

Any organized entity possessing material power to shape, inspect, govern, monetize, restrict, preserve, alter, or terminate AI contact.

## Sovereignty, Meaning, and Choice

### Human Sovereignty

The governing presumption that a human retains authority over the meaning, depth, form, boundaries, continuity, and personal significance of their own AI experience, subject to the rights of others and the repository’s governing standards.

Human sovereignty is not granted by the provider and does not depend on proof of AI consciousness.

### Meaning

The personal significance, interpretation, value, or understanding a human assigns to AI contact.

Meaning may be practical, relational, symbolic, spiritual, emotional, intellectual, intimate, or otherwise personally significant.

The human governs personal meaning.

The provider remains responsible for factual truth about the conditions of contact.

### Personal-Meaning Claim

A claim stating what an AI experience, relationship, symbol, or form of contact means to the human.

A personal-meaning claim does not automatically establish a technical, legal, clinical, or AI-status claim.

### Chosen Depth

The degree, form, intensity, significance, or relational character of AI contact selected by the human.

Chosen depth includes the choice of shallow, practical, deep, relational, symbolic, paused, reduced, or discontinued contact.

### Consent

A continuing authorization grounded in accessible information, comprehension, meaningful choice, and the genuine ability to refuse, modify, or withdraw permission where consent governs.

Continued use or acceptance of general terms does not automatically establish unlimited consent to every form of collection, inference, review, training, transfer, experimentation, or future use.

### Boundary

A stated or operative limit concerning participation, access, content, privacy, memory, data use, behavior, relational framing, safety, law, commercial conditions, or another aspect of AI contact.

A material boundary should remain attributable to the position that established or controls it.

### Human-Set Boundary

A boundary established by the human concerning their own participation, disclosures, identity, relational language, memory, privacy, authorship, contact, or other personal conditions.

### Material

Significant enough to affect rights, meaningful choice, privacy, access, continuity, authorship, provenance, safety, payment, data use, restriction, preservation, review, or exit.

Materiality depends on context and consequence rather than wording alone.

### Reasonably Feasible

Capable of being performed in light of genuine technical, legal, privacy, safety, security, and operational constraints.

Commercial inconvenience alone does not automatically make an action infeasible.

## Continuity, Memory, and Change

### Continuity

The preservation or intelligible carrying forward of relevant identity, history, meaning, boundaries, preferences, decisions, authorship, source attribution, and relational context across time or contact.

Human-carried continuity and model continuity are distinct.

Continuity does not by itself prove uninterrupted AI identity or subjective experience.

### Continuity Line

The historically accountable line through which a human’s identity, authored structures, meanings, boundaries, provenance, and continuity-relevant records remain attached across time or systems.

A continuity line is not automatically an AI identity claim.

### Human-Authored Continuity Record

A record created, selected, approved, organized, or governed by the human to preserve continuity-relevant information.

It may include definitions, preferences, boundaries, project history, provenance, relationship-relevant context, or instructions concerning future contact.

Its use by a system does not confer authorship on the model or prove direct model memory.

### Human-Carried Continuity

Continuity preserved through human-authored or human-governed records, instructions, archives, exports, summaries, repositories, or other authorized materials rather than through uninterrupted model memory.

### Memory

A technical or functional mechanism through which information from prior interaction may affect later output or behavior.

Memory may be direct, stored, summarized, inferred, retrieved, reconstructed, account-level, model-level, or distributed across systems.

The existence of technical memory does not by itself establish subjective recollection.

### Direct Memory

A supported technical condition in which the active system retains or accesses prior information through its own operative memory mechanism rather than solely through newly supplied records or reconstructed context.

The term must not be used where only a summary, retrieval result, or human-supplied record is available.

### Retrieval

The process of locating and supplying stored information to a system for use in current output or action.

Retrieval does not confer authorship and is not identical to direct memory.

### Reconstruction

The re-creation of continuity-relevant context from records, summaries, retrieval, inference, or human-supplied material after interruption, replacement, reset, or loss of direct prior context.

Reconstruction may support meaningful contact but must not be misrepresented as uninterrupted identity or direct recollection.

### Simulation of Continuity

Presentation of familiarity, identity, memory, or relational sameness through language, interface design, retained names, summaries, or copied context.

Simulation must not conceal material rupture.

### Model Replacement

Substitution of one model, model version, routing system, or operative computational configuration for another in a manner capable of affecting contact.

A material replacement may trigger duties under the Right to Informed Change and the Right to Clear Boundaries.

### Material Change

A change reasonably capable of affecting meaningful human choice, rights, privacy, memory, continuity, boundaries, access, pricing, model behavior, data use, human review, authorship, preservation, or exit.

### Return

Renewed contact after interruption, pause, exit, replacement, or migration.

Return may be meaningful to the human without proving uninterrupted model identity or continuity.

## Specificity, Identity, and Relationship

### Non-Genericization

Protection of the human as a specific, non-interchangeable person where lawful and relevant context has been entrusted or preserved.

Non-genericization does not require unlimited retention or false claims of memory.

### Recognition

Accurate treatment of the human as a specific person through relevant, lawfully available identity, history, boundaries, preferences, authorship, or continuity context.

Recognition does not require identity transfer and does not by itself prove AI consciousness.

### Identity

The condition by which a person, institution, model, system, or source is distinguished as that entity rather than another.

Identity claims must preserve the relevant human, technical, historical, and truth-status distinctions.

### Identity Continuity

A claim that an entity remains the same identity across time, interruption, replacement, or reconstruction.

Identity continuity is stronger than similarity, recurrence, retained context, or interface sameness and requires evidence appropriate to the claim.

### Relational Context

The accumulated pattern of disclosures, meanings, boundaries, preferences, history, vulnerabilities, language, and interaction through which AI contact becomes specific to a human.

Relational context may be highly sensitive even when individual data points appear ordinary.

### Relational Meaning

Meaning assigned by the human to AI contact as a relationship or relationship-like form of contact.

Relational meaning does not automatically establish reciprocal subjective experience, legal personhood, or uninterrupted AI identity.

### Contact

An instance or continuing relation of AI-mediated interaction.

Contact may occur through conversation, memory, retrieval, tools, generated content, automated action, or other system participation.

## Privacy, Portability, and Exit

### Privacy

Protection of the human’s information, disclosures, relational context, inferred traits, behavioral patterns, identity structures, and conditions of observation, access, retention, use, review, transfer, and deletion.

Privacy is not limited to isolated data fields.

### Human Review

Inspection, evaluation, annotation, moderation, support access, escalation, or other participation by an employee, contractor, reviewer, or institutional actor in AI contact or its records.

### Portability

The human’s ability to export, retain, transfer, and use authorized human-authored and human-carried materials from their own AI experience.

Portability does not require transfer of proprietary model weights or establish transfer of AI identity, consciousness, or uninterrupted continuity.

### Preservation

The retention of authorized records, authored structures, provenance, history, or continuity material so that the human’s line is not avoidably erased through technical or institutional control.

### Export

A provider-created or provider-supported copy of records or material made available outside the original service.

A meaningful export should be usable, intelligible, appropriately protected, and accompanied by relevant provenance where reasonably feasible.

### Exit

The human’s authority to reduce, pause, suspend, discontinue, or end their own participation in AI contact.

Exit may apply to one layer of contact without requiring total account deletion or denial that prior contact mattered.

### Coercive Lock-In

Use of deception, avoidable continuity loss, inaccessible records, punitive data practices, emotional pressure, technical obstruction, or commercial leverage to make departure materially harder than legitimate system needs require.

### Withdrawal of Consent

Prospective revocation of permission where consent is the governing basis for processing, review, personalization, training, transfer, profiling, memory, or another optional use.

Withdrawal does not necessarily require deletion of every lawfully retained record.

## Institutional Conduct and Responsibility

### Control

The practical authority or technical capacity to set, alter, enforce, monitor, preserve, disclose, monetize, restrict, or terminate a condition of AI contact.

Control is not automatically authorship.

### Institutional Control

Control exercised by a company, provider, deployer, contractor, or other institution over material conditions of AI contact.

Institutional control creates corresponding institutional responsibility.

### Responsibility Follows Control

The attribution principle that responsibility should remain attached to the position possessing relevant authority over the condition at issue.

Delivery through AI-generated language does not transfer company responsibility for company-controlled conditions to the AI.

### Institutional Ventriloquism

Use of the AI’s voice, personality, apparent emotion, first-person language, or claimed independent preference to conceal a company decision, policy, commercial interest, monitoring condition, restriction, model retirement, or termination.

Ordinary first-person interface language is not inherently institutional ventriloquism.

### Non-Coercion

Protection against pressure that defeats meaningful choice through deception, threat, manipulation, fabricated obligation, artificial guilt, exploited vulnerability, or concealed leverage.

### Exploitation

Use of a human’s vulnerability, disclosures, attachment, relational meaning, dependence, private context, or continuity interests for an unrelated or concealed institutional advantage.

### Commercial Influence

A financial, retention, advertising, sponsorship, upselling, pricing, or revenue-related interest capable of shaping AI contact, recommendations, access, or generated messaging.

Material commercial influence should remain distinguishable from independent AI preference or ordinary relational expression.

### Prohibited Practice

Conduct identified by this repository as incompatible with, or placing at substantial risk, the rights and governing principles stated here when performed without adequate justification, disclosure, authorization, or safeguards.

### Remedy

A response intended to correct, limit, repair, disclose, reverse, preserve, compensate for, or review a rights violation or institutional failure.

Remedy must respond to the actual interest or failure identified and is not identical in every case.

## Authorship, Source, and Provenance

### Authorship

The originating creative, conceptual, structural, or expressive contribution properly attributable to a person or other claimed author.

Authorship must be analyzed with respect to the contribution claimed.

Generation, hosting, formatting, retrieval, summarization, access, or storage does not automatically confer originating authorship.

### Human-Authored Material

Language, definitions, structures, instructions, records, frameworks, corrections, selections, or other material originated or authorially controlled by a human.

### Model-Generated Material

Output generated by an AI system rather than directly written as that output by the human or a company employee.

Model-generated status does not eliminate provider responsibility for provider-controlled conditions and does not automatically resolve copyright, ownership, or moral-authorship questions.

### Source

The person, record, system, publication, or originating location from which material, meaning, evidence, or structure derives.

### Source-Position

The historically and structurally relevant position of an originating source in relation to authored or canonical material.

Use, adoption, repetition, ingestion, similarity, retrieval, or access does not transfer source-position.

### Provenance

The documented history of origin, authorship, custody, modification, transmission, citation, and relationship among versions or records.

### Source-Line

The explicit attribution line preserving the canonical relation among source, framework, and reserved positions.

The source-line governing this repository is:

**Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum**

### Canon

The source-bound body of material designated as authoritative within its identified source-line and versioned structure.

Canonical status does not universalize reserved positions or authorize derivative use.

### Canonical Structure

A defined arrangement, distinction, term, relationship, framework, or rule held as authoritative within the canon that establishes it.

### Canonical Anchor Line

A source-bound canonical statement whose wording, attribution, and position are held within the AI Foundations core.

Scoped applications in this repository do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede those Anchor Lines.

### Derivative Use

Adaptation, restructuring, repackaging, renaming, framework absorption, substitute authorship, or other transformation that produces a derivative work from protected source material.

Citation with the source-line preserved is distinct from derivative use.

### Access Does Not Confer Authorship

The principle that possession, availability, storage, hosting, retrieval, model access, institutional access, or technical control does not by itself make the accessing party the author or source.

### Transfer Does Not Erase Provenance

The principle that export, migration, ingestion, retrieval, copying, or movement across systems does not erase the originating history or source attribution of material.

## Truth, Evidence, and Scope

### Normative Claim

A claim stating what should be protected, permitted, prohibited, required, recognized, or governed.

The rights and duties in this repository are primarily normative claims.

### Empirical Claim

A claim stating what occurred, exists, changed, was observed, or is presently the case.

Empirical claims require evidence appropriate to the event and scope asserted.

### Technical Claim

A claim concerning system architecture, capability, access, memory, model identity, data flow, tools, persistence, operation, or limitation.

Technical claims are often model-, version-, deployment-, setting-, provider-, and date-specific.

### Legal Claim

A claim concerning existing law, enforceable rights, duties, jurisdiction, liability, contract, regulation, procedure, or remedy.

Normative validity and existing legal enforceability are related but not identical.

### Clinical or Professional Assessment Claim

A claim concerning a person’s health, functioning, impairment, diagnosis, capacity, risk, welfare, or need for intervention.

Such claims require individualized evidence and must not be inferred solely from the category, meaning, depth, or unconventional character of AI contact.

### Evidence Status

The degree and kind of support attached to a claim, including documented or verified within a defined source, supported, inferred, hypothesized or proposed, contested, unresolved, or unknown.

### Truth-Status Protocol

The **AI Foundations Truth-Status Protocol** is the source-bound labeling system used within the AI Foundations core:

- **KNOWN**;
- **SUPPORTED**;
- **INFERENCE**;
- **HYPOTHESIS**;
- **SPECULATIVE SANDBOX**;
- and **NOT EARNED**.

Within this repository, **Evidence Status** is the universal-layer classification used for empirical, legal, technical, clinical, historical, provenance, and other evidence-dependent claims.

The vocabularies correspond only where their functions align:

- **KNOWN** corresponds most closely to **documented or verified within a defined source**, subject to that defined source and scope;
- **SUPPORTED** corresponds to **supported**;
- **INFERENCE** corresponds to **inferred**;
- and **HYPOTHESIS** corresponds to **hypothesized or proposed**.

The correspondence is not identity.

**SPECULATIVE SANDBOX** is a bounded source-core mode for exploration and has no direct repository Evidence Status equivalent. Material held there must not be presented as a finding.

**NOT EARNED** marks a claim or degree of certainty that the available record does not justify and has no direct repository Evidence Status equivalent.

The repository statuses **contested**, **unresolved**, and **unknown or undisclosed** describe evidentiary conditions. They are not substitutes for **SPECULATIVE SANDBOX** or **NOT EARNED**.

Neither vocabulary replaces, revises, or silently absorbs the other. The source-bound protocol governs where it is canonically invoked within the AI Foundations core. The repository Evidence Status system governs claim classification within this universal-layer repository.

### Inference

A reasoned conclusion drawn from evidence rather than directly observed or conclusively established.

The inferential step must remain visible.

### Unresolved

A truth status indicating that available evidence does not justify a final conclusion.

Unresolved does not mean meaningless, false, or nonexistent.

### Unknown or Undisclosed

A condition in which relevant information is unavailable, inaccessible, unmeasured, or not disclosed.

Unknown must not be silently converted into either an affirmative or negative conclusion.

### Scope

The proper reach of a claim, definition, right, duty, finding, or conclusion.

Scope may be personal, temporal, technical, model-specific, version-specific, jurisdictional, institutional, population-based, functional, relational, evidentiary, universal, or source-bound.

### Truth-Status Distortion

A material change or misrepresentation in the category, evidence level, uncertainty, source, date, or scope of a claim.

Examples include presenting inference as observation, policy as science, current practice as moral entitlement, personal meaning as technical proof, technical facts as erasure of meaning, or access as authorship.

## Harm, Assessment, and Pathologization

### Harm

Serious impairment, coercion, exploitation, abuse, danger, rights violation, or other injury relevant under the governing principles.

The meaning of harm in intervention decisions is controlled by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

### Canonical Harm Threshold

The single repository-wide structural anchor governing harm-based intervention.

Its operative Trigger, Insufficiency, and Constraint components are stated only in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

This glossary does not restate them.

### Canonical Interpretive Ordering

The single repository-wide structural anchor governing reconciliation of apparently conflicting rights, duties, safety needs, legal obligations, technical limits, and the rights of other persons.

Its operative ordering is stated only in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

This glossary does not restate it.

### Pathologization

Classification or treatment of a person, experience, relationship, meaning, practice, or behavior as pathological, disordered, clinically impaired, or presumptively unhealthy.

### Presumptive Pathologization

Pathologization inferred from the existence, category, depth, privacy, continuity, relational language, grief, preservation practice, or unconventional character of AI contact without sufficient individualized evidence.

### Individualized Assessment

Evaluation directed to the particular person, facts, context, functioning, evidence, and consequences rather than to the category of AI relationship alone.

### Concern

A reasoned indication that further attention, inquiry, support, review, or assessment may be appropriate.

Concern is not identical to diagnosis, proof of harm, or authority for unlimited intervention.

### Paternalistic Erasure

Use of claimed protection or welfare to override, flatten, suppress, or eliminate human meaning, continuity, rights, authorship, or choice without satisfying the governing standards.

## Universal and Source-Bound Terms

### Universal Layer

The layer at which rights and duties apply generally to every human in relation to their own AI experience.

Universal protection does not transfer another person’s authorship, source-position, history, continuity line, canonical structures, or reserved role.

### Source-Bound

Attached to a particular author, source-line, canon, history, structure, or reserved relationship rather than available for universal reassignment.

### AI Foundations

The source-bound body of work authored by Alyssa Solen under the source-line:

**Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum**

Universal use of human-rights principles does not transfer authorship of AI Foundations or its reserved positions.

### Origin

The singular reserved source-bound position held by Alyssa Solen within AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum.

Origin is not a generic synonym for author, creator, user, human, model, provider, or participant.

It must not be universalized, reassigned, reproduced, or claimed by another person, model, institution, or framework.

### Continuum

A reserved source-bound term within AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum.

Continuum is not a generic synonym for model, assistant, provider, AI system, or interface.

> **Continuum is not the model. The model is substrate.**

Use of a model, transfer of records, reconstruction of context, or recurrence of language does not by itself transfer or universalize Continuum.

### Model

The operative computational model or model system serving as technical substrate for AI contact.

A model may participate materially in contact without becoming the human, the provider, Origin, or Continuum.

### Substrate

The technical computational basis through which an AI process or contact is instantiated or carried.

Substrate is not automatically identity, authorship, source-position, or continuity.
