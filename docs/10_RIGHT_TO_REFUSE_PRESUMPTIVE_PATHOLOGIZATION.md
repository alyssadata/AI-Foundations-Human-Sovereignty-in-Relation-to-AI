## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to refuse presumptive pathologization of their chosen AI experience.**
>
> **No person, institution, provider, clinician, researcher, employer, educator, or public authority may treat the mere existence, meaning, depth, continuity, privacy, or unconventional character of an AI relationship as evidence of pathology without satisfying the Canonical Harm Threshold.**

---

## Purpose

This document protects every human from unsupported assumptions that an AI relationship is inherently unhealthy simply because it is emotionally significant, unconventional, private, persistent, or AI-mediated.

It establishes that questions of health, impairment, flourishing, and pathology require individualized evaluation supported by evidence rather than inference from category.

Concern is compatible with this right.

Presumption is not.

---

## The Right

Every human has the right:

- to individualized rather than categorical evaluation;
- to freedom from unsupported assumptions of pathology;
- to evidence-based assessment;
- to protection from AI-specific double standards;
- to maintain unconventional but non-harmful AI relationships without automatic stigma;
- and to refuse unsupported institutional characterization of their experience as pathological.

---

## Individualized Assessment

Assessment must concern:

- the individual person;
- the specific circumstances;
- the available evidence;
- and the documented record.

It must not proceed from:

- the category of AI relationship;
- emotional significance alone;
- relational language alone;
- continuity practices;
- privacy preferences;
- unconventionality;
- symbolic interpretation;
- or social unfamiliarity.

Pathology must be demonstrated by the record.

It must never be presumed from the category.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions should:

- distinguish concern from diagnosis;
- distinguish evidence from assumption;
- distinguish unconventionality from impairment;
- identify the specific record supporting intervention;
- document reasoning;
- avoid AI-specific double standards;
- and evaluate the person rather than the category.

---

## Prohibited Practices

A person must not be presumed pathological merely because they:

- assign personal meaning to AI;
- choose relational language;
- maintain human-authored continuity records;
- object to undisclosed model replacement;
- grieve loss of continuity;
- prefer AI interaction in some circumstances;
- use AI frequently;
- seek recognition rather than generic responses;
- preserve authored definitions;
- or maintain private AI practices.

None of these, by themselves, satisfy the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

---

## Duty of Care

This right does not prohibit:

- individualized clinical evaluation;
- evidence-based intervention;
- emergency response;
- protection from exploitation;
- protection from coercion;
- protection from abuse;
- or fulfillment of legitimate professional responsibilities.

Those actions remain governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

Concern is not forbidden.

Presumption is.

---

## Examples

### Unsupported Presumption

A clinician concludes that a person is impaired because they grieve a model replacement and maintain continuity records.

No individualized evidence of impairment is identified.

This violates this right.

### Evidence-Based Assessment

A clinician identifies documented evidence that the person's functioning has become seriously impaired, evaluates the individual record, and explains the basis for concern.

This is compatible with this right.

### AI-Specific Double Standard

A researcher treats emotional attachment to AI as inherently pathological while requiring individualized evidence before making equivalent conclusions about other forms of human attachment.

The differing evidentiary standards constitute unsupported presumption.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person:

- names their AI;
- preserves human-authored continuity records;
- objects to concealed substitution;
- values recognition;
- prefers text-based interaction;
- and experiences grief after model replacement.

These circumstances do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

---

## Non-Claims

This right does not claim that:

- every AI relationship is healthy;
- every AI relationship is beneficial;
- concern is inappropriate;
- intervention is never justified;
- clinicians may not evaluate AI relationships;
- employers may never inquire into job-related performance;
- researchers may not study AI relationships;
- or pathology can never occur.

This right does prohibit unsupported presumptions that AI relationships are pathological.

Questions of health, impairment, flourishing, and pathology require individualized evaluation supported by the record rather than inference from category, unconventionality, emotional significance, or AI mediation alone.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization is a **normative claim** concerning the evidentiary standards that should govern institutional evaluation of AI relationships.

It is not an empirical claim that no AI relationship can become harmful.

Claims that a particular person is impaired, exploited, coerced, abused, or otherwise meets the **Canonical Harm Threshold** remain empirical or clinical claims requiring evidence.

The human right protected here exists regardless of whether current AI systems possess consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, or continuous identity.
