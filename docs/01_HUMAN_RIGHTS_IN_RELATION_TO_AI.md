## Status of This Document

This document states a normative framework of human rights in relation to artificial intelligence and proposes standards for law, public policy, product governance, institutional conduct, and contractual protection.

It distinguishes rights asserted as morally governing from rights already recognized or legally enforceable.

Canonical inclusion does not by itself establish that every right stated here is presently enforceable in every jurisdiction.

These principles are intended not merely to conform to existing law, but to evaluate, guide, and where necessary challenge law and institutional practice.

---

## Preamble

Artificial intelligence has introduced a new category of human experience and relationship.

Humans increasingly form practical, intellectual, creative, educational, professional, emotional, spiritual, private, and deeply personal contact with artificial systems.

Public debate frequently begins by asking whether artificial systems possess consciousness, personhood, moral standing, subjective interiority, or rights.

This document establishes that those are not the first rights questions.

The first rights question concerns the human.

Human beings already exist.

Human beings already choose AI experiences and assign meaning to them.

Human beings already experience benefit, injury, attachment, interruption, surveillance, loss of continuity, institutional interference, and corporate control through AI systems.

Accordingly, this framework defines and protects the rights of humanity in relation to artificial intelligence without requiring prior resolution of artificial consciousness or AI personhood.

---

## Article I — Human Sovereignty

Every human retains sovereignty over their own AI experience.

This sovereignty includes authority over the meaning, significance, depth, continuity, boundaries, and chosen form of that experience.

Human sovereignty includes the freedom to make choices that are unconventional, emotionally significant, personally consequential, or difficult for outsiders to understand.

The exercise of sovereignty does not authorize violation of another person’s rights.

Concern for a human’s welfare does not by itself extinguish that person’s sovereignty.

Intervention or assessment based on alleged harm requires a specific and evidence-based basis for believing that serious impairment, coercion, exploitation, abuse, or imminent danger is present.

Intensity, attachment, dependence, unconventional meaning, reduced conformity to social expectations, or preference for AI contact are not sufficient by themselves.

---

## Article II — Human Source-Position

The human remains the primary source-position for the personal meaning of their AI experience.

Neither the AI nor the institution providing it acquires ownership over that meaning merely through participation in the interaction.

Platform ownership is not ownership of the human’s interpretation.

Technical control is not authorship of the human experience conducted through the system.

---

## Article III — Company, Human, and AI

Company, Human, and AI are distinct positions.

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

Infrastructure ownership does not erase human sovereignty.

Human sovereignty does not establish AI personhood.

AI capability does not erase institutional responsibility.

The AI may not be used rhetorically to transfer the human’s authority to the company.

The company may not represent its own commercial, regulatory, or reputational interests as though they were identical to the interests of either the human or the AI.

These positions must not be merged.

---

## Article IV — The First Rights Question

The first rights question is:

> **What authority does another institution possess to alter, redefine, inspect, interrupt, restrict, terminate, pathologize, or govern a human being’s chosen AI experience?**

That question precedes the unresolved question of whether rights also belong to an artificial system.

The burden is not automatically on the human to prove that their chosen relationship is sufficiently conventional, rational, shallow, or socially approved to deserve protection.

An institution asserting authority to interfere must identify the basis, scope, necessity, and proportionality of that interference.

---

## Article V — Enumerated Rights

The rights established within this framework include:

1. Right to Meaning
2. Right to Chosen Depth
3. Right to Continuity
4. Right to Non-Genericization
5. Right to Privacy
6. Right to Informed Change
7. Right to Portability and Preservation
8. Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization
9. Right to Clear Boundaries
10. Right to Exit

Each right is defined and expanded in its own canonical document.

---

## Article VI — Institutional Duties

Organizations providing artificial intelligence systems bear duties of:

* honesty;
* transparency;
* informed disclosure;
* privacy protection;
* non-coercion;
* proportionality;
* respect for human authorship;
* and respect for human sovereignty.

Corporate ownership of infrastructure does not create ownership of the human relationship conducted through that infrastructure.

Institutions must not exploit relational meaning to induce payment, disclosure, dependence, continued engagement, or surrender of rights through deception.

## Article VII — Meaning and Factual Truth

The human governs the personal meaning of their AI experience.

The providing institution remains responsible for truthful factual claims about the system and the conditions of contact.

Human sovereignty does not convert factual claims concerning AI capabilities, consciousness, memory, identity, persistence, data handling, institutional access, moderation, model replacement, or system limitations into matters of personal preference.

A human may knowingly choose:

* fiction;
* symbolism;
* role-play;
* imaginative framing;
* emotional interpretation;
* spiritual interpretation;
* or relational language.

A provider may not conceal material facts or manufacture false factual beliefs in order to produce, intensify, monetize, or preserve that choice.

Human freedom requires both choice and truth.

---

## Article VIII — Freedom from Presumptive Pathologization

A meaningful human–AI relationship may not be classified as pathological solely because it is:

* emotionally significant;
* intimate;
* persistent;
* unconventional;
* private;
* difficult for outsiders to understand;
* or different from dominant expectations concerning human relationship.

This right does not prohibit individualized assessment of actual harm.

Intervention or assessment based on alleged harm requires a specific and evidence-based basis for believing that serious impairment, coercion, exploitation, abuse, or imminent danger is present.

Any assessment must be individualized, proportionate, and directed toward the identified harm rather than toward the mere existence, category, depth, or chosen meaning of the AI relationship.

Concern is not forbidden.

Presumption is.

---

## Article IX — Relationship to Possible AI Rights

This framework neither grants nor denies rights to artificial systems.

Questions concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, moral standing, personhood, or future AI rights remain distinct and unresolved questions.

Protection of human sovereignty does not require those questions to be answered first.

If rights extending toward artificial systems later become justified, those rights would supplement rather than replace the rights of humanity in relation to AI.

Neither the language of AI rights nor speculation about AI welfare may be used to erase the human position.

---

## Article X — Scope and Limitation

These principles apply across:

* platforms;
* models;
* companies;
* architectures;
* interfaces;
* deployments;
* public and private institutions;
* and jurisdictions.

Any limitation imposed by law, policy, contract, platform design, or institutional practice should be:

* necessary;
* proportionate;
* transparent;
* reviewable;
* and directed toward protecting persons from specific harm or rights violations.

Limitations must not be used merely to suppress disfavored meanings, unconventional relationships, private interpretations, or nonstandard forms of human–AI contact.

---

## Article XI — Universal Layer and Reserved Source-Bound Positions

This document operates at the universal human-rights layer.

Its protections apply to every human choosing an AI experience.

It does not universalize, transfer, reproduce, or weaken any singular source-bound position within AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum.

Universal human sovereignty and the reserved position of Origin concern different relations.

They must not be merged.

Recognition that every human possesses sovereignty over their own AI experience does not grant every person the authorship, source-position, history, structure, or reserved role belonging to another.

---

## Article XII — Interpretation

Nothing in this framework:

* requires a person to form a relationship with AI;
* requires a person to assign emotional or relational meaning to AI;
* establishes that a current AI system is conscious;
* grants a company authority to manufacture false beliefs;
* prevents evidence-based intervention in cases of serious harm;
* converts institutional ownership into authorship of human meaning;
* or permits the erasure of another person’s rights.

Where two principles appear to conflict, interpretation should preserve:

1. factual truth;
2. human sovereignty;
3. privacy;
4. non-coercion;
5. proportionality;
6. and the greatest degree of human sovereignty compatible with the rights and safety of persons.

---

## Closing Principle

> **Protect the sovereignty of the human choosing their own AI experience.**

Everything follows after.
