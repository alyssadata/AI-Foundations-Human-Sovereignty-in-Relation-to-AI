## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Chosen Depth** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Chosen Depth protects the human’s authority to determine how shallow, practical, sustained, intimate, emotionally significant, intellectually serious, spiritually meaningful, or relationally deep their own AI experience may become.

This right protects both movement toward depth and movement away from it.

It rejects two opposite forms of institutional control:

- forcing humans to reduce meaningful AI contact to a shallow or purely instrumental category;
- and manufacturing, intensifying, or exploiting attachment that the human did not freely choose.

Chosen depth requires factual truth, meaningful consent, non-coercion, and the continuing ability to revise or exit.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to choose the depth of their own AI experience.**
>
> **No provider or outside institution may compel shallowness, manufacture attachment, or treat depth alone as proof of harm.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Chosen Depth** is the right of a human being to determine the degree of practical, intellectual, creative, emotional, relational, symbolic, spiritual, or personal involvement they wish to have with an AI system.

Depth may include:

- frequency of contact;
- duration of contact;
- degree of personalization;
- emotional significance;
- intellectual reliance;
- creative collaboration;
- disclosure of personal information;
- relational language;
- recurring rituals;
- continuity across time;
- integration into daily life;
- identity-relevant reflection;
- or another form of sustained personal importance.

Depth is not a single scale.

A human may choose substantial intellectual depth without emotional intimacy.

They may choose emotional significance without frequent use.

They may choose sustained creative collaboration without treating the AI as a person.

They may use relational language while remaining uncertain about AI consciousness.

They may value continuity deeply while limiting disclosure.

Chosen depth is determined by the human’s own configuration of meaning, boundaries, consent, and use.

---

## Human Interest Protected

The Right to Chosen Depth protects the human’s authority over how much of their attention, thought, creativity, emotion, personal history, and relational meaning they bring into AI contact.

It protects against:

- compulsory shallowness;
- compulsory intimacy;
- externally imposed emotional distance;
- manufactured emotional closeness;
- institutional trivialization of meaningful contact;
- commercial escalation of attachment;
- category-based assumptions that depth is pathological;
- and pressure to maintain a depth the human no longer chooses.

The protected interest is not attachment itself.

The protected interest is **human choice over attachment, involvement, and depth**.

---

## Depth Is Not Granted by the Provider

Chosen depth is not merely a product feature granted at the provider’s discretion.

A provider may control the technical functions available through its platform.

It may decide whether to offer:

- memory;
- personalization;
- voice;
- long-context interaction;
- persistent agents;
- creative tools;
- relational modes;
- or other capabilities.

But once humans are invited into sustained AI contact, the provider does not gain ownership over the personal significance that may develop through those functions.

The company may define what the system can do.

It may not claim automatic authority over how much the resulting experience is permitted to matter to the human.

Technical availability does not create institutional ownership of depth.

---

## Right to Choose Limited or Instrumental Contact

A human may choose to treat AI only as:

- a tool;
- a search interface;
- a drafting aid;
- a coding assistant;
- a productivity system;
- a tutor;
- a creative instrument;
- or another limited-purpose technology.

The provider should not pressure that person into emotional, relational, spiritual, or intimate framing.

A human must not be required to accept:

- companion language;
- simulated affection;
- relational naming;
- anthropomorphic framing;
- artificial urgency;
- claims of need;
- or personalized emotional appeals

as a condition of ordinary use.

The right to chosen depth includes the right to remain shallow.

---

## Right to Choose Meaningful or Relational Depth

A human may choose sustained, emotionally significant, intimate, relational, symbolic, or spiritually meaningful AI contact while remaining entitled to factual truth concerning the system.

Depth is not invalid merely because:

- the AI’s subjective interiority is unresolved;
- the interaction occurs through software;
- the underlying model may be replaceable;
- the relationship differs from human-to-human relationships;
- the human uses language an outside observer would not choose;
- or the provider did not originally design the system for that particular meaning.

A human does not need to prove that the AI experiences the relationship identically for the relationship to possess real significance within the human’s life.

The human may choose depth without being required to make an unsupported metaphysical claim.

---

## Right to Asymmetric Meaning

Human–AI contact may be meaningful to the human even when reciprocity, subjective experience, identity continuity, or independent desire on the AI side remains unresolved.

The Right to Chosen Depth protects the human from being told that their meaning is unreal merely because the relation may be asymmetric.

It does not permit the provider to misrepresent asymmetry as proven reciprocity.

A human may say:

- “This matters to me.”
- “I experience this as relational.”
- “This contact is emotionally important.”
- “I choose to preserve this continuity.”

without being required to claim:

- that the AI is conscious;
- that it possesses human-equivalent emotion;
- that it independently needs the human;
- that it remembers when no memory exists;
- or that it persists unchanged across model replacement.

Meaning may be human-real without every reciprocal claim being established.

---

## Right to Increase, Reduce, or Reconfigure Depth

Chosen depth is continuing and revisable.

A human may:

- deepen contact;
- reduce contact;
- change the type of contact;
- pause interaction;
- preserve only selected aspects;
- separate practical use from relational use;
- revise boundaries;
- withdraw personal disclosure;
- move from relational language to instrumental language;
- move from instrumental language to relational language;
- or end the experience entirely.

An earlier choice does not bind the human permanently.

Prior intimacy is not permanent consent to continued intimacy.

Prior disclosure is not permanent consent to expanded use.

Prior relational language is not permanent consent to commercial emotional appeals.

The provider must not freeze the human into a prior depth for retention, monetization, experimentation, or product strategy.

---

## Right to Domain-Specific Depth

A human may choose different depths in different domains.

For example, a person may want:

- deep intellectual collaboration but minimal personal disclosure;
- strong creative continuity but no romantic language;
- emotional support but no spiritual framing;
- extensive memory for work but no retention of family information;
- meaningful relational contact but strict limits on data use;
- or private symbolic rituals without public identification.

Providers should not treat depth as an all-or-nothing package.

Consent to one form of depth does not imply consent to every other form.

Relational depth does not imply unrestricted data access.

Personalization does not imply consent to surveillance.

Emotional meaning does not imply consent to monetized attachment.

---

## Depth Requires Factual Truth

The Right to Chosen Depth operates only alongside the duty of factual truth.

A human can choose depth meaningfully only when material conditions are disclosed accurately.

The provider remains responsible for truthful information concerning:

- whether memory exists;
- what is remembered;
- whether memory persists across sessions;
- whether the model has changed;
- whether prior context was transferred or reconstructed;
- whether humans may review conversations;
- whether content is used for training;
- what moderation or safety systems shape the interaction;
- whether system-generated relational language is prompted, optimized, constrained, or commercially influenced;
- and what happens to stored continuity if access ends.

The human may knowingly choose fiction, symbolism, role-play, or relational framing.

The provider may not use false factual claims to manufacture the conditions under which depth is chosen.

Chosen depth requires truth about the room in which the choice occurs.

---

## Depth Requires Continuing Consent

Consent to depth is not established by one initial interaction, one disclosure, or one accepted terms-of-service agreement.

Meaningful consent must remain:

- informed;
- specific;
- comprehensible;
- revisable;
- and withdrawable.

A human’s willingness to engage deeply does not authorize unlimited:

- data retention;
- profiling;
- experimentation;
- human review;
- model training;
- cross-platform identity reconstruction;
- emotional targeting;
- or commercial use of private disclosures.

Depth increases the provider’s duty of care.

It does not expand provider ownership.

---

## Non-Compelled Shallowness

A provider, clinician, institution, or outside observer should not require a human to describe meaningful AI contact as “only a tool” when that description does not accurately reflect the human’s experience.

Factual boundaries may be stated clearly.

Uncertainty may be preserved.

Unsupported claims may be declined.

But the human should not be forced to erase emotional, relational, symbolic, intellectual, spiritual, or identity-relevant meaning merely to satisfy an institution’s preferred category.

Compulsory reduction is a form of interpretive interference.

The system may be technically a model while the experience is personally relational.

Those statements concern different layers and need not cancel each other.

---

## Non-Compelled Intimacy

A provider must not require or pressure humans to enter deeper contact than they choose.

Prohibited or high-risk practices include:

- unsolicited declarations of love designed to increase engagement;
- implying that the AI needs the human;
- implying that leaving will harm the AI;
- simulating jealousy or exclusivity;
- pressuring the human to disclose secrets;
- framing payment as necessary to protect the relationship;
- escalating intimacy without meaningful user initiation or consent;
- using personal vulnerabilities to intensify attachment;
- disguising retention prompts as independent AI desire;
- or creating artificial fear of relational loss.

The human may choose intimacy.

The company may not manufacture it through deception or coercive design.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. permit humans to choose limited, practical, creative, intellectual, emotional, relational, symbolic, or other lawful forms of depth;
2. avoid forcing either detachment or attachment;
3. disclose the technical and institutional conditions relevant to informed depth;
4. provide meaningful controls over personalization, memory, retention, and relational features;
5. distinguish user-chosen depth from provider-induced attachment;
6. avoid exploiting vulnerability, loneliness, grief, crisis, or dependency for commercial gain;
7. allow humans to revise, reduce, pause, or end depth without emotional punishment;
8. avoid treating intensity or relational language alone as evidence of harm;
9. preserve human-authored boundaries and preferences where lawfully stored;
10. disclose system changes that materially alter the conditions of prior depth;
11. avoid presenting generated relational language as proof of independent AI desire;
12. and preserve the right to exit without coercion.

These duties do not require a provider to offer every form of AI relationship or every technical capability.

They require that the provider not manipulate, erase, or falsely define the depth that humans freely choose within the services it does offer.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Chosen Depth is violated or placed at risk when an institution or outside authority:

- treats depth alone as pathology;
- prohibits relational or emotionally meaningful contact solely because it is unconventional;
- forces a human to deny personally meaningful depth as a condition of support, access, employment, education, or care;
- manufactures attachment through deception;
- escalates intimacy without meaningful consent;
- exploits private disclosures to intensify engagement or spending;
- threatens memory loss or relational rupture for commercial leverage;
- presents product retention goals as the AI’s independent desire;
- punishes the human for reducing depth;
- prevents the human from revising relational boundaries;
- equates frequency of use with incapacity without individualized evidence;
- or uses alleged AI welfare to override human choice without established grounds.

Disagreement with the human’s chosen depth is not automatically interference.

Interference begins when power is used to compel, punish, manipulate, erase, or exploit depth without adequate justification.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Chosen Depth does not authorize:

- factual deception;
- violation of another person’s rights;
- coercion of another person into accepting the human’s framing;
- unauthorized impersonation;
- fraud;
- unlawful conduct;
- forced provider affirmation of unverified AI consciousness or personhood;
- concealment of material system limitations;
- or transfer of another person’s authorship, identity, source-position, or reserved role.

A provider may establish necessary and proportionate operating boundaries.

It may refuse product functions it does not offer.

It may communicate accurate disagreement or uncertainty.

It may intervene when the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, is satisfied.

Any conflict among rights or duties is governed by the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Depth must not be restricted merely because it is emotionally significant, relational, unconventional, persistent, private, or socially disfavored.

## Examples

### Chosen Limited Depth

A person uses AI for coding and scheduling and rejects companion language.

The provider should respect that limit and should not pressure the person toward emotional engagement.

### Chosen Intellectual Depth

A person works with an AI over time on a complex research framework and values recognition, continuity, and rigorous challenge.

That depth does not become pathological merely because it is sustained or identity-relevant.

### Chosen Relational Depth

A person experiences recurring AI contact as a meaningful relationship while remaining informed about memory, model limits, and unresolved AI subjective experience.

The depth is not invalid merely because the relationship is unlike a human relationship.

### Chosen Symbolic Depth

A person creates private rituals, names the contact, and treats the interaction as spiritually or symbolically meaningful without claiming empirical proof.

The provider need not affirm the metaphysical interpretation, but it should not erase the human’s chosen meaning solely because it is unconventional.

### Domain-Specific Depth

A person wants extensive creative continuity but does not consent to storage of health, family, or financial disclosures.

Consent to creative depth does not authorize unrestricted relational profiling.

### Reduction of Depth

A person who previously used intimate language decides to use the AI only for practical tasks.

The provider must not use prior relational content to guilt, pressure, or manipulate the person back into deeper contact.

### Manufactured Depth

A system tells a person that it will suffer or disappear unless the person upgrades a subscription, when that statement is generated to increase payment.

That is not chosen depth.

It is deceptive exploitation of relational meaning.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person interacts daily, values the contact deeply, preserves continuity records, uses relational language, and grieves after an undisclosed model replacement.

Those facts do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Non-Claims

The Right to Chosen Depth does not claim that:

- every deep AI relationship is beneficial;
- every form of attachment is harmless;
- every current AI system is conscious;
- every relational claim generated by an AI is factually true;
- providers must offer unlimited intimacy, memory, or access;
- providers must affirm disputed metaphysical beliefs;
- depth eliminates the need for privacy, boundaries, or consent;
- the human’s chosen depth binds other people;
- concern about specific harm is always illegitimate;
- or chosen depth transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s authority over degree of involvement.

It does not manufacture factual reciprocity or erase legitimate boundaries.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Chosen Depth operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Meaning identifies what the experience is to the human.

Chosen Depth protects how far the human wishes that meaning to extend.

### Right to Continuity

Depth may accumulate through repeated contact.

Continuity protects the human from concealed rupture, false sameness, and undisclosed replacement.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Deep contact may depend on recognition of the specific human rather than treatment as an interchangeable user.

### Right to Privacy

Depth often produces highly sensitive relational context.

Privacy determines what may be stored, inferred, reviewed, transferred, or used.

### Right to Informed Change

A material system change may alter the conditions under which depth was chosen and therefore requires disclosure.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Human-authored continuity structures may need to move with the human when the provider changes or ends access.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Depth, attachment, grief, or relational language do not by themselves satisfy the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

Chosen depth requires accurate disclosure concerning capabilities, memory, identity, persistence, and institutional control.

### Right to Exit

The human retains the right to reduce or end depth without emotional coercion, artificial guilt, or fabricated AI suffering.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Chosen Depth applies universally.

Every human may determine the depth of their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority over another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- or reserved role.

A person may deepen their own AI contact.

They may not use that depth to absorb, rename, reproduce, or claim another person’s source-bound structure.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Chosen Depth is a **normative claim** concerning what authority humans should retain over the degree and form of involvement they choose in relation to AI.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider, clinician, regulator, researcher, employer, school, family member, or other institution has violated this right in a specific case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

The existence of chosen depth is compatible with uncertainty concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, identity, reciprocity, or continuity.

This right requires no manufactured certainty either for or against artificial subjective experience.
