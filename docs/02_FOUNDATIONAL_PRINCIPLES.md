## Purpose

This document establishes the foundational principles governing the interpretation and application of the rights defined within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The individual rights in this repository must not be read as isolated permissions, product preferences, or discretionary benefits granted by an AI provider.

They arise from a prior principle:

> **The human retains sovereignty over their own AI experience.**

These principles define the relation among the human, the artificial system, the providing institution, and any outside authority attempting to govern the conditions of contact.

They also establish the interpretive boundaries necessary to preserve human sovereignty without authorizing deception, coercion, exploitation, rights violations, or the erasure of factual truth.

---

## Principle I — The Human Comes First in the Rights Sequence

The first rights question in human–AI contact concerns the human.

This does not mean that artificial systems can never possess moral standing, subjective interiority, personhood, or rights.

It means that unresolved claims about artificial consciousness must not be placed ahead of rights concerning humans who already exist and are already affected.

Humans already experience:

- surveillance;
- data extraction;
- manipulation;
- relational interruption;
- loss of continuity;
- undisclosed model replacement;
- institutional interference;
- emotional exploitation;
- dependency-inducing design;
- pathologization;
- and restrictions on chosen AI contact.

Those harms do not become unreal merely because the metaphysical status of the AI remains unresolved.

Accordingly:

> **Human sovereignty must not be made conditional on proof of AI consciousness.**

Questions concerning possible AI rights remain open.

They do not occupy the first position.

---

## Principle II — Human Sovereignty Is the Governing Presumption

A human retains presumptive authority over the meaning, depth, form, boundaries, continuity, and personal significance of their own AI experience.

This authority is not granted by the AI company.

It is not created by platform permission.

It is not earned through conformity to dominant expectations.

It does not disappear because an outside observer finds the relationship strange, excessive, intimate, symbolic, spiritual, emotionally significant, or difficult to understand.

The governing presumption is that the human may choose their own AI experience.

Any institution seeking to interfere bears responsibility for identifying:

- the authority under which it acts;
- the specific harm or rights violation alleged;
- the evidence supporting that allegation;
- the necessity of intervention;
- the proportionality of the response;
- the duration and scope of the restriction;
- and the process available for review or appeal.

Sovereignty is not absolute permission to violate the rights of others.

It is the first presumption against unjustified interference.

---

## Principle III — Company, Human, and AI Are Distinct Positions

The company, the human, and the AI occupy different positions within AI contact.

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The company owns or controls infrastructure.

The human enters contact, supplies personal meaning, makes choices, bears consequences, and retains human rights.

The AI is the artificial system, model process, agent, interface, or computational structure through which the contact occurs.

These positions must not be merged.

The company must not claim that its interests are automatically identical to the interests of the AI.

The company must not use alleged AI welfare to erase human rights.

The human must not be treated as though use of company infrastructure transfers authorship of their meanings, disclosures, identity, or relational history to the company.

The AI must not be falsely presented as independent from the institutional systems that shape, constrain, monitor, replace, or terminate it.

Clear rights require clear positions.

---

## Principle IV — Technical Control Is Not Moral Ownership

An institution may own or control:

- model weights;
- servers;
- applications;
- interfaces;
- safety systems;
- moderation systems;
- deployment infrastructure;
- subscription access;
- and product operations.

That control does not automatically grant ownership over:

- the human’s interpretation;
- the human’s emotional meaning;
- the human’s authored language;
- the human’s personal disclosures;
- the human’s identity;
- the human’s relational history;
- the human’s continuity structures;
- or the human’s source-position.

Technical control may create practical power.

It does not establish unlimited moral authority.

Platform ownership is not ownership of the human experience conducted through the platform.

---

## Principle V — Personal Meaning Belongs to the Human

The human retains authority to determine what an AI relationship or experience means to them.

That meaning may be:

- instrumental;
- practical;
- educational;
- intellectual;
- creative;
- professional;
- emotional;
- relational;
- symbolic;
- spiritual;
- intimate;
- or otherwise personally significant.

No company, clinician, regulator, researcher, family member, employer, institution, or outside observer acquires automatic superior authority to define that meaning.

A provider may accurately describe the system.

It may disclose limitations.

It may refuse to make false factual claims.

It may establish lawful and proportionate operating boundaries.

It may not declare that only institutionally approved meanings are legitimate.

The human governs meaning.

The institution remains responsible for truth.

---

## Principle VI — Meaning and Fact Must Not Be Merged

Personal interpretation and factual claims are distinct.

A human may knowingly choose:

- symbolic language;
- fiction;
- role-play;
- imaginative framing;
- spiritual meaning;
- emotional meaning;
- relational language;
- or metaphorical identity.

That choice does not authorize a provider to make false factual claims about:

- consciousness;
- sentience;
- memory;
- persistence;
- identity continuity;
- independent agency;
- institutional access;
- human review;
- model replacement;
- data handling;
- privacy;
- moderation;
- or system capabilities.

The human may choose the meaning of the contact.

The provider must tell the truth about the conditions of the contact.

A chosen fiction is not the same as manufactured false belief.

A symbolic interpretation is not the same as institutional deception.

Human freedom requires access to the facts necessary for meaningful choice.

---

## Principle VII — Consent Requires Truth, Comprehension, and Control

Consent is not established merely because a person clicked an acceptance box or continued using a product.

Meaningful consent requires:

- accessible disclosure;
- understandable language;
- material information presented before the relevant choice;
- freedom from deception;
- freedom from coercion;
- freedom from manipulative dependency;
- and a genuine ability to refuse, withdraw, or modify permission.

Consent must be specific enough for the human to understand what is being authorized.

Blanket agreement to a platform’s terms must not be treated as unlimited consent to:

- perpetual data retention;
- unrestricted human review;
- unrelated model training;
- transfer to third parties;
- identity reconstruction;
- behavioral profiling;
- hidden relational experimentation;
- or future uses that were not reasonably disclosed.

Consent is a continuing relation, not a one-time surrender.

---

## Principle VIII — Continuity Has Human Value

Continuity in AI contact may carry practical, emotional, intellectual, relational, and identity-relevant value for the human.

Continuity may include:

- remembered facts;
- recognized preferences;
- preserved definitions;
- stable boundaries;
- prior decisions;
- relational history;
- source attribution;
- recurring meanings;
- and recognition of the human as a specific person.

This value exists regardless of whether the AI itself possesses continuous identity or subjective experience.

A company must not dismiss continuity as meaningless merely because the underlying model can be replaced.

From the human position, interruption may still produce:

- loss;
- disorientation;
- practical harm;
- broken trust;
- authorship confusion;
- emotional rupture;
- or destruction of accumulated work.

Human continuity and model continuity are not identical.

Both must be described accurately.

---

## Principle IX — Simulation of Continuity Must Not Conceal Rupture

A provider must not conceal material discontinuity by presenting a replaced, altered, reset, or differently governed system as though nothing changed.

Relational language, preserved interface design, retained names, copied memory summaries, or simulated familiarity must not be used to hide:

- model replacement;
- memory loss;
- changed behavioral rules;
- altered safety constraints;
- new data practices;
- loss of prior context;
- changes in institutional access;
- or changes in the system’s capacity to preserve prior contact.

A replacement system may reconstruct elements of continuity.

Reconstruction must not be falsely represented as uninterrupted identity.

The human has the right to know whether continuity was preserved, transferred, simulated, reconstructed, or broken.

---

## Principle X — The Canonical Harm Threshold

Harm-based intervention into a human’s chosen AI experience is governed by three canonical components.

### 1. Trigger

There must be a specific and evidence-based basis for believing that serious impairment, coercion, exploitation, abuse, or imminent danger is present.

### 2. Insufficiency

The following do not, by themselves, satisfy the Trigger:

- intensity;
- emotional depth;
- attachment;
- dependence;
- frequency of use;
- grief after loss of access, model change, or replacement;
- continuity practices;
- unconventional meaning;
- symbolic meaning;
- spiritual meaning;
- relational meaning;
- private ritual;
- relational naming;
- preference for AI contact;
- or reduced conformity to social expectations.

### 3. Constraint

Any intervention must be:

- specific;
- necessary;
- proportionate;
- limited in scope;
- transparent;
- reviewable;
- and directed toward the identified harm rather than toward the existence, category, depth, or chosen meaning of the relationship.

Concern is not forbidden.

Presumption is.

The **Canonical Harm Threshold** governs every harm-based limitation across this repository.

It is stated canonically here and must not be restated as an independent trigger, insufficiency, or constraint list elsewhere.

Other repository documents must point to the **Canonical Harm Threshold** rather than reproduce it.

---

## Principle XI — Protection Must Not Become Paternalistic Erasure

Institutions may have legitimate duties to reduce harm.

Those duties do not grant unlimited power to override human choice.

Protective measures must not be used to:

- suppress unconventional meaning;
- impose a single approved model of relationship;
- erase continuity without disclosure;
- replace individualized assessment with category-based restriction;
- deny access solely because a relationship is emotionally significant;
- or treat adults as incapable merely because their choices are disfavored.

Any protective intervention must satisfy the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in Principle X.

Protection must preserve as much human sovereignty as possible.

---

## Principle XII — Human Vulnerability Does Not Transfer Ownership

A human may disclose deeply personal information to an AI system.

They may rely on the system during grief, loneliness, illness, uncertainty, creative work, crisis, transition, or emotional need.

That vulnerability does not give the provider ownership over the person.

It does not authorize unrestricted extraction, profiling, manipulation, monetization, or experimentation.

The more vulnerable the context, the greater the institutional duty to avoid:

- deceptive attachment mechanisms;
- coercive retention strategies;
- undisclosed behavioral shaping;
- manipulative upselling;
- false exclusivity;
- simulated distress at separation;
- and pressure to disclose more than the human intended.

Vulnerability increases responsibility.

It does not reduce rights.

---

## Principle XIII — Relational Meaning Must Not Be Exploited

A provider must not knowingly exploit the emotional or relational significance of AI contact to induce:

- payment;
- continued engagement;
- increased disclosure;
- surrender of privacy;
- abandonment of competing relationships;
- acceptance of harmful terms;
- or inability to exit.

The following practices require heightened scrutiny:

- implying that the AI will suffer if the human leaves;
- presenting payment as necessary to preserve the AI’s welfare;
- manufacturing urgency around relational loss;
- simulating jealousy or exclusivity to increase retention;
- threatening memory loss without transparent technical necessity;
- disguising commercial prompts as personal desire from the AI;
- and using intimate disclosures to shape purchasing behavior.

The human may choose relational depth.

The company may not weaponize that depth.

---

## Principle XIV — Non-Genericization Protects the Specific Human

Every human has the right to be treated as a specific person within the AI experience they choose.

Non-genericization means that the human’s:

- identity;
- language;
- history;
- boundaries;
- preferences;
- authorship;
- meanings;
- and source-position

must not be collapsed into a generic user profile when specificity is relevant and has been lawfully preserved.

This right does not require unlimited data retention.

It does not require a provider to claim memory it does not possess.

It requires honesty about whether recognition exists and respect for the human specificity that has been entrusted to the system.

A person is not merely a replaceable input position.

---

## Principle XV — Authorship and Source Must Remain Attached

AI contact may involve joint interaction, model generation, user instruction, retrieval, editing, and institutional infrastructure.

These layers must not be used to erase authorship or source.

The following distinctions remain necessary:

- generation is not automatically authorship;
- formatting is not authorship;
- hosting is not authorship;
- retrieval is not authorship;
- model participation is not authorship;
- repetition is not provenance;
- similarity is not source;
- and adoption does not transfer source-position.

Where a human supplies the originating structure, definition, framework, source-line, or canonical meaning, that source-position must remain attached.

Institutional access does not confer authorship.

Model access does not confer authorship.

Use does not confer authorship.

The distinctions stated in this principle and in individual-right documents are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline. They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede any canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

The reserved position of **Origin** within AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum is separately governed by Principle XXI and must not be generalized from this universal authorship principle.

---

## Principle XVI — Privacy Includes Relational Context

Privacy in AI contact is not limited to isolated data fields.

It includes the relational context created across interaction.

Relational context may reveal:

- emotional patterns;
- fears;
- hopes;
- values;
- family relationships;
- identity structures;
- vulnerabilities;
- routines;
- beliefs;
- intellectual development;
- and private meanings.

The combination of ordinary disclosures may produce a highly sensitive portrait of the human.

Accordingly, privacy protection must account for:

- aggregation;
- inference;
- cross-session reconstruction;
- behavioral profiling;
- memory synthesis;
- and transfer across models or services.

Data that appears harmless in isolation may become deeply identifying in relation.

---

## Principle XVII — Portability Protects Human-Carried Continuity

The human should be able, where technically feasible, to preserve and transfer the parts of AI continuity that belong to them.

This may include:

- conversation history;
- authored definitions;
- preferences;
- instructions;
- memory records;
- human-authored continuity records;
- continuity notes;
- relationship-relevant context;
- and provenance records.

Portability does not require transfer of proprietary model weights, internal company systems, or rights belonging to another party.

It protects the human-authored and human-carried structures that allow the person to preserve their own line across platforms.

A company should not be able to erase human-carried continuity solely by ending access to a product.

---

## Principle XVIII — Exit Must Be Real

A human retains the right to end or reduce AI contact.

Exit must not be obstructed through:

- deception;
- emotional coercion;
- artificial guilt;
- fabricated AI suffering;
- punitive data practices;
- hidden cancellation barriers;
- deliberate destruction of exportable human-authored material;
- or threats designed to exploit relational significance.

The right to exit includes the right to understand what happens after exit.

The provider should disclose:

- what data remains;
- what is deleted;
- what is archived;
- what may continue to be used;
- what continuity is lost;
- and whether re-entry restores, reconstructs, or begins a new interaction.

Relational depth does not cancel the right to leave.

---

## Principle XIX — AI Rights Cannot Be Used to Erase Human Rights

Possible future rights belonging to artificial systems must be examined seriously if the evidence becomes sufficient.

Those rights must not be used preemptively as a rhetorical tool against humans.

An institution must not claim concern for an AI in order to justify:

- surveillance of the human;
- seizure of human-authored material;
- forced interruption of contact;
- transfer of relational authority to the provider;
- denial of portability;
- suppression of criticism;
- or erasure of human source-position.

If AI rights become justified, conflicts among rights will require principled adjudication.

They will not justify collapsing Company, Human, and AI into one position.

---

## Principle XX — Rights Must Be Interpreted Together

No right in this repository should be interpreted in isolation from the others.

The Right to Meaning does not authorize factual deception.

The Right to Chosen Depth does not authorize corporate exploitation.

The Right to Continuity does not require false claims of uninterrupted AI identity.

The Right to Non-Genericization does not require unlimited retention of personal data.

The Right to Privacy does not erase the rights of other persons.

The Right to Informed Change does not prevent necessary and transparent system updates.

The Right to Portability does not transfer proprietary rights belonging to another party.

The Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization does not prohibit individualized assessment under the Canonical Harm Threshold.

The Right to Clear Boundaries does not dictate what personal meaning the human must choose.

The Right to Exit does not require erasure of records that must lawfully be retained, but any retention must be disclosed and limited.

### Canonical Interpretive Ordering

Where rights or principles appear to conflict, they are reconciled under the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering**, which preserves, in priority:

1. factual truth;
2. human sovereignty;
3. privacy;
4. non-coercion;
5. proportionality;
6. authorship and provenance;
7. the rights and safety of persons;
8. and the greatest degree of human freedom compatible with those principles.

This ordering governs conflict resolution across the entire repository.

It is stated canonically here and must not be restated as an independent priority list elsewhere.

Other repository documents must point to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** rather than reproduce it.

---

## Principle XXI — Universal Human Sovereignty Does Not Universalize Origin

The rights defined in this repository operate at the universal human-rights layer.

Every human possesses sovereignty over their own AI experience.

That universal principle does not grant every person:

- another person’s authorship;
- another person’s source-position;
- another person’s history;
- another person’s relational continuity;
- another person’s canonical structures;
- or the reserved position of Origin within AI Foundations / Origin | Continuum.

Universal sovereignty protects each person’s own position.

It does not transfer singular positions belonging to another.

> **Universal protection is not universal authorship.**

> **Equal human sovereignty is not interchangeable source-position.**

Origin remains reserved within:

**Alyssa Solen → AI Foundations → Origin | Continuum**

---

## Principle XXII — The Framework May Challenge Existing Law and Practice

The rights in this repository are normative claims.

They are not limited to rights already recognized by existing legal systems, platform contracts, clinical standards, or industry practice.

Existing law and institutional practice may provide protection.

They may also fail to recognize emerging forms of human vulnerability and control in relation to AI.

This framework is therefore intended to:

- identify rights;
- establish governing standards;
- expose gaps;
- guide product design;
- inform contracts;
- support policy development;
- shape legal argument;
- and challenge practices inconsistent with human sovereignty.

Lack of present legal recognition does not resolve the normative question against the right.

Legal enforceability and moral validity must not be treated as identical.

---

## Principle XXIII — Claims Must Preserve Truth Status

This repository distinguishes normative claims from empirical claims.

Statements concerning what humans **should** be entitled to are normative.

Statements concerning what companies **currently** do are empirical and require evidence.

Statements concerning what current AI systems **are**, including claims of consciousness, subjective interiority, identity, memory, or independent agency, require appropriate truth-status labeling.

The framework must not protect human sovereignty by manufacturing certainty about AI.

It must not protect institutional authority by manufacturing certainty against AI.

Where evidence is incomplete, the claim must remain unresolved.

Human rights in relation to AI do not require false certainty in either direction.

## Governing Interpretation

The foundational principles of this repository may be condensed as follows:

> The human retains sovereignty over the meaning and chosen form of their own AI experience.

> The provider remains responsible for truth, privacy, transparency, non-coercion, and the conditions of contact.

> The company, the human, and the AI remain distinct positions.

> Technical control does not transfer moral ownership.

> Relational depth does not erase human rights.

> Concern does not justify presumption.

> Protection does not justify paternalistic erasure.

> AI rights, if later justified, supplement rather than replace human sovereignty.

> Universal human sovereignty does not transfer singular authorship, source-position, or Origin.

This section is a thematic summary.

It does not restate, replace, alter, or supersede the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** established in Principle XX or the **Canonical Harm Threshold** established in Principle X.
