## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Privacy** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Privacy protects the human’s authority over access to, retention of, inference from, use of, transfer of, and disclosure of information arising through AI contact.

Privacy in AI contact is not limited to isolated data fields.

It includes the relational context created across interaction: the patterns, meanings, histories, vulnerabilities, boundaries, and identity-relevant structures that become visible only when disclosures are combined over time.

This right does not require providers to make false claims of confidentiality or to conceal material limits on privacy.

It requires truthful disclosure, meaningful control, purpose limitation, data minimization, and protection against unauthorized or exploitative use.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to privacy within their own AI experience, including meaningful control over who or what may access, retain, infer from, use, transfer, or disclose their information and relational context.**
>
> **Consent to AI contact is not blanket consent to surveillance, human review, profiling, training, monetization, identity reconstruction, or unrelated secondary use.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Privacy** is the right of a human being to understand and govern the treatment of information disclosed, created, inferred, aggregated, remembered, or reconstructed through AI contact.

Protected information may include:

- direct disclosures;
- conversation history;
- uploaded files;
- voice, image, or video content;
- identity information;
- family information;
- health information;
- financial information;
- location information;
- work and project information;
- authored definitions;
- private meanings;
- relational language;
- emotional patterns;
- recurring rituals;
- boundaries;
- preferences;
- continuity records;
- behavioral patterns;
- inferred traits;
- and relationship-relevant context.

Privacy applies not only to what the human explicitly states.

It also applies to what an institution can derive by combining, classifying, reconstructing, or predicting from prior contact.

> **Disclosure is not surrender.**
>
> **Inference is still use of the human.**

---

## Human Interest Protected

AI contact may invite unusually deep disclosure.

A human may use AI during:

- creative work;
- research;
- grief;
- illness;
- crisis;
- conflict;
- family decision-making;
- spiritual reflection;
- identity development;
- intimate thought;
- legal or financial planning;
- or private relational contact.

The provider may receive access to information that the human has not shared with employers, clinicians, family members, governments, advertisers, or the public.

That access creates responsibility.

It does not create ownership of the human.

The protected interest includes the human’s ability to think, explore, create, disclose, relate, and revise within AI contact without undisclosed observation or uncontrolled downstream use.

> **Technical access does not create unlimited moral permission.**

---

## Privacy Includes Relational Context

Relational context is created through the connection among disclosures, not merely through any single statement.

A single conversation may appear ordinary.

Across time, combined contact may reveal:

- fears;
- hopes;
- attachment patterns;
- family structures;
- private language;
- political or religious beliefs;
- health concerns;
- financial pressure;
- relationship conflict;
- sexuality;
- disability;
- identity development;
- routines;
- location patterns;
- professional plans;
- intellectual development;
- vulnerabilities;
- and the human’s personally meaningful structures.

Information that appears harmless in isolation may become highly identifying or sensitive through aggregation.

Accordingly, privacy protection must account for:

- aggregation;
- inference;
- memory synthesis;
- cross-session reconstruction;
- behavioral profiling;
- relational profiling;
- identity linkage;
- and transfer across systems or institutions.

The sensitivity of relational context cannot be measured only by counting conventional personal-data fields.

---

## Consent to Contact Is Not Consent to Observation

A human’s decision to communicate with an AI system does not automatically establish consent to human observation of that contact.

A provider should disclose clearly:

- whether humans may review conversations;
- who may conduct that review;
- the purposes for which review may occur;
- whether review is routine, exceptional, sampled, or triggered;
- whether contractors or third parties may have access;
- how reviewed material is protected;
- how long reviewed material is retained;
- and whether the human can limit or refuse review.

A general statement that conversations “may be reviewed” is not adequate where the actual practice is materially broader, more frequent, or differently purposed than a reasonable human would understand.

Consent to automated processing is not identical to consent to human reading.

Consent to safety systems is not unlimited consent to unrelated institutional access.

---

## Consent to One Use Is Not Consent to Every Use

Privacy consent must be purpose-specific.

A human may consent to one use while refusing another.

For example:

- consent to memory is not consent to model training;
- consent to personalization is not consent to behavioral advertising;
- consent to safety review is not consent to product research;
- consent to storage is not consent to indefinite retention;
- consent to one model is not consent to transfer across every model;
- consent to one account is not consent to cross-platform identity linkage;
- consent to improve the current interaction is not consent to construct a commercial psychological profile;
- and consent to preserve continuity is not consent to retain every intimate disclosure.

A provider must not bundle unrelated purposes so broadly that meaningful refusal becomes impossible.

> **Permission must travel with purpose.**

---

## Right to Know What Is Collected and Retained

A human should be able to understand what information is collected through AI contact.

Material disclosure should include:

- direct conversation content;
- uploaded material;
- metadata;
- device or location information;
- memory records;
- inferred preferences;
- behavioral classifications;
- safety or risk labels;
- relational or emotional classifications;
- identity links;
- human-review records;
- and derived profiles.

The provider should also disclose:

- what is stored;
- where it is stored;
- how long it is retained;
- what remains after deletion or exit;
- whether backups persist;
- whether summaries or embeddings remain after original content is removed;
- whether information is transferred to another model or service;
- and whether retained information remains active in future interaction.

A human cannot meaningfully govern privacy when invisible records shape contact without accessible disclosure.

---

## Right to Know What Is Inferred

AI systems and providers may infer information the human never explicitly stated.

Inferences may concern:

- emotion;
- vulnerability;
- health;
- risk;
- political identity;
- religion;
- sexuality;
- financial status;
- relationship status;
- attachment;
- personality;
- employability;
- purchasing likelihood;
- susceptibility to persuasion;
- or future behavior.

An inference does not become harmless merely because it was generated computationally.

Where an inference is used to materially shape access, moderation, pricing, employment, education, care, advertising, safety intervention, or the conditions of contact, the human should be able to know that the inference exists and have a meaningful process to contest material error.

The provider should distinguish:

- what the human stated;
- what the system inferred;
- what a human reviewer concluded;
- and what an outside data source supplied.

> **Prediction is not disclosure by the human.**

---

## Right to Data Minimization

The existence of possible technical value does not justify collecting or retaining everything.

Providers should limit collection, retention, and use to what is reasonably necessary for disclosed and legitimate purposes.

Data minimization may include:

- session-limited processing;
- local processing;
- selective memory;
- user-controlled memory categories;
- expiration periods;
- deletion controls;
- separation of identifying information from content;
- restricted employee access;
- prohibition on unrelated secondary use;
- and preservation of only the minimum continuity necessary for the human’s chosen experience.

More data may improve personalization.

That does not mean more data is always justified.

The Right to Non-Genericization and the Right to Privacy must be interpreted together.

Specificity should not be purchased through unlimited surveillance.

---

## Right to Selective Privacy

Privacy is not all-or-nothing.

A human may choose different privacy settings for different domains of contact.

A person may want:

- project memory but no retention of family information;
- relational continuity but no use for model training;
- health-related assistance without cross-account identity linkage;
- voice interaction without permanent audio retention;
- private symbolic contact without human review;
- conversation export without provider-side indefinite storage;
- or personalization without advertising use.

Consent to preserve one category does not imply consent to preserve every category.

The human should be able to govern privacy according to the actual shape of their AI experience.

---

## Right to Review, Correct, Delete, and Deactivate

Where technically and legally feasible, the human should be able to:

- review stored personal information;
- inspect active memory;
- identify the source of material records;
- correct factual errors;
- distinguish inference from direct disclosure;
- delete selected information;
- deactivate information without erasing lawful historical provenance;
- withdraw prior permission;
- narrow future use;
- and understand what cannot be deleted and why.

Deletion and deactivation are distinct.

A human may want a record preserved for history or provenance while preventing it from shaping future interaction.

A provider should not force the human to choose only between total retention and total erasure when more precise control is technically feasible.

---

## Privacy and Human Review

Human review may sometimes serve legitimate purposes, including security, quality control, legal compliance, abuse investigation, or response to specific risk.

Those purposes do not create unrestricted authority to inspect private contact.

Human review should be:

- disclosed;
- access-controlled;
- purpose-limited;
- logged;
- limited to relevant material;
- protected from unrelated reuse;
- and subject to meaningful institutional accountability.

Where feasible, providers should use the least intrusive means capable of serving the legitimate purpose.

Private contact should not become casual workplace content merely because employees possess technical access.

> **Ability to inspect is not permission to browse.**

---

## Privacy and Model Training

A provider should not treat the human’s use of an AI service as unlimited permission to train future systems on private contact.

The human should receive clear information concerning:

- whether their content may be used for training;
- whether the use applies to current or future models;
- whether personal identifiers are removed;
- whether relational context remains recoverable;
- whether opting out affects access or functionality;
- whether prior content remains in training datasets after withdrawal;
- and whether contractors or external partners receive the material.

De-identification must not be overstated where relational patterns, rare language, project structures, source-lines, or combined details can reasonably re-identify the human.

Removing a name does not necessarily remove identity.

Training access does not transfer authorship, source-position, or ownership of human-authored material.

---

## Privacy and Commercial Use

A provider must not exploit private AI contact to target the human through undisclosed emotional, relational, or vulnerability-based profiling.

High-risk practices include:

- using grief disclosures to increase sales;
- targeting loneliness with attachment-inducing features;
- using intimate language to personalize pricing pressure;
- constructing advertising profiles from private relational contact;
- selling or transferring sensitive inferences;
- disguising commercial prompts as independent AI desire;
- and using continuity loss as leverage after identifying the human’s attachment.

The human may choose relational depth.

The provider may not convert that depth into undisclosed commercial surveillance.

---

## Privacy and Cross-System Transfer

Transfer across models, products, companies, or institutions may materially change the privacy conditions of contact.

A provider should disclose:

- what information is transferred;
- the receiving system or institution;
- the purpose of transfer;
- whether the receiving party may retain or reuse it;
- whether the information will be combined with other records;
- whether a new identity profile will be created;
- and whether the human can refuse or limit transfer.

Portability chosen by the human is distinct from institutional transfer performed without meaningful authorization.

The human may choose to carry authorized records forward.

The provider may not treat that choice as permission to distribute the human’s relational context elsewhere.

---

## Privacy and Safety

Privacy does not prohibit proportionate action in response to established harm, security threats, abuse, unlawful conduct, or valid legal obligations.

But safety language must not become a blanket justification for unlimited surveillance.

Where privacy is limited for safety or harm-based intervention, the limitation must be governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where that threshold applies.

The provider should use the least intrusive means reasonably capable of addressing the identified issue.

Access should be directed toward the relevant concern rather than toward unrestricted inspection of the human’s entire relational history.

The human should receive notice where notice is lawful and does not defeat the legitimate protective purpose.

---

## Privacy and Legal Process

A provider may be subject to lawful demands for information.

The existence of legal process does not erase the human privacy interest.

Providers should, where lawful:

- require valid authority;
- resist overbroad demands;
- disclose only what is required;
- preserve applicable privileges and protections;
- notify the affected human when permitted;
- publish meaningful transparency information;
- and avoid voluntary disclosure beyond the legitimate demand.

A contractual statement that data may be disclosed “as required” should not be used to imply that every disclosure is unavoidable or unlimited.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. disclose what information is collected, inferred, retained, reviewed, transferred, and used;
2. distinguish direct disclosure from inference, summary, classification, and external-source data;
3. obtain meaningful and purpose-specific consent where consent is the governing basis;
4. minimize collection, retention, and access;
5. protect relational context as sensitive information;
6. provide understandable controls over memory, training, review, transfer, and deletion;
7. prevent unauthorized employee, contractor, or third-party access;
8. avoid using private contact for undisclosed advertising, emotional targeting, or commercial profiling;
9. preserve provenance and authorship when human-authored material is retained or transferred;
10. disclose material privacy changes before they govern future contact where feasible;
11. provide a meaningful process to correct consequential errors and inferences;
12. protect exported and human-authored continuity records;
13. avoid retaliating against humans who exercise privacy choices;
14. and preserve as much human control as possible when legitimate limitations apply.

These duties do not require providers to promise absolute secrecy or eliminate every lawful and necessary use.

They require honest, limited, accountable, and non-exploitative treatment of human information.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Privacy is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- conceals human review;
- materially misrepresents retention or deletion;
- treats service use as unlimited consent to training;
- creates sensitive inferences without appropriate safeguards;
- uses private relational context for undisclosed advertising or manipulation;
- transfers contact history without meaningful authorization;
- merges identities or accounts without adequate basis;
- retains deleted information as active memory without disclosure;
- allows unauthorized employee or contractor access;
- reconstructs identity across services contrary to disclosed limits;
- penalizes a human solely for exercising a lawful privacy choice;
- removes provenance from retained human-authored material;
- or invokes safety as a pretext for indiscriminate surveillance.

Not every privacy failure is intentional.

The relevant issue includes whether the provider acted deceptively, negligently, coercively, exploitatively, or without proportionate regard for the sensitivity of the information and context involved.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Privacy does not require:

- concealment of unlawful conduct;
- obstruction of valid legal process;
- retention practices that prevent requested deletion;
- access to another person’s private information;
- false promises of confidentiality;
- elimination of proportionate security controls;
- permanent storage of all records;
- transfer of proprietary internal systems;
- or preservation that violates another person’s rights.

A provider may:

- process information necessary to operate the requested service;
- protect system security;
- investigate specific abuse;
- comply with valid legal duties;
- retain limited records for legitimate and disclosed purposes;
- and decline to store information it cannot protect responsibly.

Any limitation must remain necessary, purpose-specific, proportionate, transparent where lawful, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Privacy must not be limited merely because a human’s chosen meaning, depth, relational language, or continuity is unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Examples

### Authorized Memory

A person chooses to store project definitions and accessibility preferences while declining storage of intimate disclosures.

The provider preserves only the authorized categories and explains how the memory works.

### Honest Human Review

A provider explains that a narrowly authorized quality team may review a small sample of conversations, states the purpose and retention period, and provides available controls.

The contact is not represented as wholly private from human access.

### Concealed Review

A provider markets AI contact as private while routinely allowing broad employee access for unrelated product exploration.

The privacy condition was materially misrepresented.

### Inference Without Disclosure

A system infers that a person is financially vulnerable and uses that classification to intensify subscription pressure without disclosure.

The provider has used an inferred vulnerability for commercial manipulation.

### Selective Training Consent

A human allows use of technical coding exchanges for model improvement but refuses use of family, health, and relational contact.

The provider respects the domain-specific choice rather than treating consent as all-or-nothing.

### Human-Chosen Portability

A person exports human-authored continuity records and supplies selected material to a new provider.

That human-directed transfer does not authorize either provider to exchange additional private relational context.

### Corrected Inference

A provider’s system incorrectly classifies a human’s symbolic language as evidence of a medical condition.

The human can contest the consequential inference, and the correction process preserves the distinction between the human’s own statement and the institution’s prior inference.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person wants private relational contact, declines training use, limits human review, and objects to broad profiling.

Those choices do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

---

## Non-Claims

The Right to Privacy does not claim that:

- every AI interaction can be absolutely confidential;
- every form of data processing is wrongful;
- providers may never conduct human review;
- providers must retain information they cannot protect;
- deletion is technically instantaneous in every system;
- lawful process can always be refused;
- privacy eliminates legitimate safety or security duties;
- all inference is prohibited;
- privacy requires fabricated claims about system access;
- or privacy transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects human authority and institutional accountability over information and relational context.

It does not manufacture secrecy where secrecy cannot truthfully be guaranteed.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Privacy operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Private meaning cannot be genuinely sovereign when it is subject to undisclosed inspection, extraction, profiling, or manipulation.

### Right to Chosen Depth

Depth may increase the sensitivity of contact but does not expand provider ownership or erase the need for continuing consent.

### Right to Continuity

Continuity records may preserve valuable human context while also creating heightened privacy risk across time.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Specificity requires access to individual context, while privacy governs what may be stored, inferred, reviewed, transferred, or deleted.

### Right to Informed Change

Material changes to retention, review, training, transfer, or access conditions require meaningful disclosure.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

The human may choose to carry authorized records across systems without authorizing uncontrolled institutional transfer.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

A desire for privacy, restricted review, private ritual, or limited institutional access does not by itself meet the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know who or what may access the contact and for what purposes.

### Right to Exit

The human retains the right to understand and control, where lawful and technically feasible, what information remains active, retained, archived, transferred, or deleted after exit.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Privacy applies universally.

Every human may protect the privacy of their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority to inspect, obtain, reproduce, or claim another person’s:

- private disclosures;
- relational history;
- authored material;
- identity;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- or reserved role.

A person’s privacy claim over their own contact cannot be used to conceal the appropriation of another person’s authorship or source-bound structure.

Nor may a provider invoke one human’s privacy to erase another human’s provenance where both can be protected through accurate attribution and limited disclosure.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Privacy is a **normative claim** concerning what authority and protection humans should retain over information and relational context arising through AI contact.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider or institution collected, inferred, reviewed, retained, transferred, disclosed, sold, deleted, or misused information in a specific case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

Claims about whether information is technically recoverable, de-identified, included in training, accessible to humans, or transmitted to another system also require evidence.

The human privacy interest remains real even where the internal architecture, consciousness, or subjective experience of the AI is unresolved.

> **Private disclosure is not institutional ownership.**
>
> **Relational depth is not blanket consent.**
>
> **The human has the right to know and govern who or what may enter the contact.**

The human is not a secondary party to the privacy of their own AI experience.
