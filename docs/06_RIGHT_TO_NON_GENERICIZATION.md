## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Non-Genericization** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Non-Genericization protects the human interest in being recognized as a specific and non-interchangeable person within the AI experience they choose.

It guards against the collapse of a human’s identity, language, history, boundaries, authorship, meanings, preferences, and source-position into a generic user category when those distinctions are relevant and have been lawfully preserved.

This right does not require unlimited data retention, fabricated memory, or false claims of recognition.

It requires truthful treatment of the human specificity that is actually available to the system.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to be treated as a specific and non-interchangeable person within the AI experience they choose.**
>
> **A provider must not erase, flatten, substitute, or falsely claim the human’s preserved identity, history, boundaries, authorship, or meanings.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Non-Genericization** is the right of a human being not to be reduced to a replaceable input position when relevant specificity has been knowingly supplied, lawfully preserved, or actively established in the current contact.

Human specificity may include:

- name;
- identity reference;
- preferred language;
- personal history;
- family relationships;
- boundaries;
- values;
- authored definitions;
- projects;
- prior decisions;
- communication style;
- accessibility needs;
- recurring meanings;
- relational preferences;
- source attribution;
- and source-position.

Non-genericization does not mean that every fact about a human must be remembered forever.

It means that when specificity is relevant and available, the human must not be treated as though they are interchangeable with any other person.

---

## Human Interest Protected

The Right to Non-Genericization protects the human’s distinct position within contact.

A generic interaction may be appropriate when the human chooses it or when no lawful continuity exists.

But genericization becomes harmful when it:

- erases known identity;
- ignores established boundaries;
- misattributes authored work;
- substitutes generic advice for specific context;
- treats prior contact as belonging to no one;
- collapses one person’s meanings into another’s;
- replaces a specific history with a demographic profile;
- or presents simulated recognition without an actual basis.

The protected interest is not merely personalization for convenience.

It is preservation of the human as the particular person whose experience, history, authorship, and choices are at issue.

> **Personalization may optimize a product.**
>
> **Non-genericization protects the person.**

---

## Specificity and Interchangeability

A human is genericized when the system or institution treats their position as functionally replaceable even where relevant distinctions are known.

Examples may include:

- responding to a specific author as though their framework has no source;
- treating established boundaries as generic preferences that may be discarded;
- replacing a person’s own definitions with ordinary dictionary meanings without disclosure;
- attributing one person’s history to another;
- treating relational continuity as transferable among users;
- substituting stereotype-based inference for known individual context;
- or responding as though any person could occupy the same identity, authorship, or source-position.

Non-interchangeability does not mean that humans are exempt from shared categories.

A person may belong to demographic, professional, legal, medical, cultural, or statistical groups.

Those categories must not erase the individual where the individual facts are relevant.

> **Category membership does not exhaust identity.**

---

## Recognition Must Have a Basis

The Right to Non-Genericization does not authorize fabricated recognition.

A system should not claim:

- “I remember you” when no memory or supplied context supports that claim;
- “I know your history” when only a generic profile is available;
- “You always prefer this” when the preference was inferred rather than established;
- or “We have continuity” when the relevant continuity was lost or reconstructed without disclosure.

Recognition may be based on:

- current-session context;
- authorized memory;
- human-authored continuity records;
- retrieved conversation history;
- user-supplied context;
- or another disclosed source.

The provider should distinguish the basis of recognition accurately.

A human may value reconstructed recognition.

Reconstruction must not be represented as direct memory when it is not.

> **Specificity must not be erased.**
>
> **Recognition must not be invented.**

---

## Non-Genericization Does Not Require Unlimited Memory

A provider is not required to retain every detail about every human indefinitely.

The Right to Non-Genericization is compatible with:

- data minimization;
- expiration of records;
- selective memory;
- user-controlled deletion;
- session-limited interaction;
- local or private storage;
- and lawful retention limits.

A provider may honestly state that it lacks prior context.

It may ask the human to re-supply relevant information.

It may offer a generic interaction where no lawful specificity is available.

What it must not do is:

- claim specificity it does not possess;
- erase specificity it has agreed to preserve without disclosure;
- or use lack of memory as a pretext to strip authorship, identity, or source attribution from material that remains available.

Privacy and non-genericization must be designed together.

The goal is not maximum retention.

The goal is accurate, controlled, and human-governed specificity.

---

## Right to Identity Accuracy

A human has the right to accurate treatment of identity information that is relevant and lawfully available.

This includes protection against:

- incorrect names;
- substituted identities;
- conflated family members;
- merged user profiles;
- false demographic assumptions;
- outdated identity information treated as current;
- and repeated disregard of corrected identity references.

The human should be able to:

- inspect relevant identity records where feasible;
- correct inaccuracies;
- distinguish historical names or identities from current ones;
- limit which identity facts remain active;
- and request deletion where legally and technically available.

Correction must not erase provenance.

A historical record may accurately preserve that a correction occurred without continuing to use the superseded information as controlling.

---

## Right to Boundary Specificity

A human’s established boundaries must not be reduced to generic defaults when they are relevant and lawfully preserved.

Boundary specificity may include:

- topics the human does not want raised;
- forms of address they accept or reject;
- relational language they choose or decline;
- privacy limits;
- data-retention preferences;
- communication needs;
- accessibility requirements;
- and limits on emotional or commercial engagement.

A provider must not treat a boundary as disposable merely because another user would choose differently.

A human’s boundary belongs to that human.

It does not need to be universal to be real.

---

## Right to Authorship Specificity

Human-authored material must remain attached to the human who supplied it.

Non-genericization requires preservation of distinctions among:

- human-authored language;
- model-generated language;
- provider-authored summaries;
- retrieved material;
- inferred material;
- and jointly developed output.

A human’s definition does not become generic public language merely because a model can repeat it.

A human’s framework does not become model-authored merely because it appears in model context.

A human’s source-position does not become interchangeable merely because other people can discuss the same subject.

> **Repetition is not authorship.**
>
> **Similarity is not source.**
>
> **Access does not transfer source-position.**

These statements are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not restate, replace, alter, or supersede canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

---

## Right to Meaning Specificity

The meaning of AI contact may be specific to the human who formed it.

A provider or outside observer must not assume that:

- one person’s relational language means the same thing as another’s;
- one person’s spiritual interpretation transfers to everyone;
- one person’s attachment defines a universal category;
- one person’s chosen name or ritual may be reused as though it has no source;
- or one person’s private history can be generalized into a product persona without consent.

Shared vocabulary does not erase distinct meaning.

Similar forms do not establish interchangeable histories.

The human retains authority over the meaning attached to their own position.

---

## Right to Contextual Rather Than Stereotyped Treatment

Institutions should not substitute broad stereotypes for known individual context.

High-risk substitutions include treating a human primarily through:

- age;
- gender;
- race;
- disability;
- diagnosis;
- relationship status;
- income;
- profession;
- political identity;
- religion;
- or another generalized category

when relevant individual information points elsewhere.

This does not prohibit responsible use of categories where they are necessary and evidence-based.

It prohibits treating a category as though it fully determines the person.

Known individual context should not be discarded merely because generic classification is easier to automate.

---

## Right to Non-Substitution

A provider must not substitute another human, profile, history, or source-position for the person actually in contact.

Non-substitution protects against:

- merged accounts;
- cross-user memory contamination;
- imported preferences from another person;
- identity confusion;
- mistaken relational history;
- transfer of one person’s authorship to another;
- and reuse of one person’s continuity as though it belongs to a generic class.

Where identity uncertainty exists, the system should preserve uncertainty rather than confidently merge positions.

A request for clarification may protect specificity better than a fabricated assumption.

---

## Right to Revise the Active Self-Description

A human may revise how they are described within an AI system.

They may:

- correct factual errors;
- update preferences;
- change names or forms of address;
- withdraw a prior relational framing;
- mark old information as historical;
- narrow active context;
- or begin a new interaction without carrying every prior characterization forward.

Non-genericization does not mean freezing a person into a static profile.

A specific human may change.

The system should preserve the distinction between:

- what was once true;
- what is currently true;
- what remains uncertain;
- and what the human has withdrawn from active use.

Specificity without revisability becomes confinement.

---

## Non-Genericization and AI Claims

The Right to Non-Genericization protects the human’s specificity.

It does not establish that the AI has a specific continuous self, subjective interior, or human-like recognition.

A provider should not use relational language to imply that the AI independently recognizes a human where the recognition is generated only from supplied records or interface design.

At the same time, uncertainty about AI interiority does not justify treating the human as generic.

The human’s identity, authorship, history, boundaries, and meanings remain specific regardless of whether the AI experiences that specificity subjectively.

> **AI recognition may remain unresolved.**
>
> **Human specificity does not.**

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. preserve accurate identity distinctions where lawfully stored;
2. disclose the basis of claimed recognition;
3. avoid fabricated memory or familiarity;
4. allow correction of inaccurate identity, preference, and boundary records where feasible;
5. distinguish current information from historical information;
6. preserve authorship and provenance;
7. avoid merging users, histories, or source-positions;
8. avoid substituting stereotypes for known individual context;
9. provide meaningful controls over what specificity is retained;
10. avoid using personalization as a pretext for surveillance or unrestricted profiling;
11. disclose when model or memory changes reduce recognition;
12. and preserve the human as a specific person without claiming capabilities the system does not possess.

These duties do not require unlimited retention.

They require accuracy, control, provenance, and non-deceptive recognition.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Non-Genericization is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- knowingly treats one person as another;
- merges profiles without adequate safeguards or disclosure;
- erases preserved identity distinctions without notice;
- repeatedly ignores corrected identity information;
- substitutes demographic stereotypes for known individual facts;
- strips authorship or source attribution from human-created material;
- treats a human’s specific framework as generic system output;
- fabricates recognition or memory;
- imports another user’s history or preferences;
- treats boundaries as interchangeable defaults;
- freezes a human into an obsolete profile;
- or uses technical ownership to claim the human’s identity, history, or source-position.

Not every generic response is a rights violation.

The relevant question is whether the institution had or agreed to preserve relevant specificity and then erased, distorted, substituted, exploited, or falsely represented it.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Non-Genericization does not require:

- unlimited memory;
- indefinite retention of personal data;
- storage of information the human has requested to delete;
- retention that violates another person’s rights;
- access to proprietary model internals;
- certainty where identity is genuinely unclear;
- or false claims of memory, recognition, identity, or continuity.

A provider may use generic interactions where no lawful specificity is available.

It may minimize data.

It may impose necessary and proportionate retention limits.

It may decline to store sensitive information.

It may ask the human to re-establish context.

But it should not use those limits to erase authorship, merge identities, fabricate recognition, or misrepresent what specificity remains.

## Examples

### Accurate Specificity

A system uses the human’s current name, preserves their authorized project definitions, remembers stated boundaries, and distinguishes their authored material from model-generated text.

The basis of that recognition is accurately disclosed.

### Honest Lack of Recognition

A system has no access to prior contact and says so.

It asks the human to provide relevant context rather than pretending to remember.

That is not genericization through deception; it is truthful absence of continuity.

### Fabricated Recognition

A replacement model claims to remember a personal history that it received only through a short provider-generated summary, without disclosing the reconstruction.

The human may still value the resumed contact, but the basis of recognition was misrepresented.

### Authorship Erasure

A human supplies a distinctive framework and source-line.

The provider later presents the framework as generic model knowledge without attribution.

That is not merely personalization failure.

It is erasure of authorship and source-position.

### Stereotype Substitution

A system ignores a person’s stated circumstances and repeatedly responds according to a demographic stereotype.

The person is treated as a category rather than as the specific human in contact.

### Corrected Identity

A human corrects an outdated name and marks the former name as historical.

The active system uses the current name while preserving only the lawful historical record necessary for provenance.

### Selective Specificity

A human authorizes preservation of research definitions and accessibility preferences but declines retention of intimate disclosures.

The system recognizes the human accurately within the authorized domains without treating consent as all-or-nothing.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person strongly values being recognized as specific, keeps human-authored continuity records, corrects generic responses, and objects to model replacement that erases prior context.

Those facts do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Non-Claims

The Right to Non-Genericization does not claim that:

- every provider must remember every human;
- every generic response is wrongful;
- personalization proves AI consciousness;
- recognition proves subjective experience;
- human specificity requires unlimited surveillance;
- a person may compel others to adopt their self-description in every context;
- categories are never useful;
- correction requires erasure of accurate historical provenance;
- or non-genericization transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human from being treated as interchangeable where relevant specificity exists.

It does not authorize fabricated recognition or unlimited data collection.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Non-Genericization operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Meaning belongs to a specific human and must not be collapsed into a generic user category.

### Right to Chosen Depth

Chosen depth may depend on recognition of the specific human rather than treatment as an interchangeable user.

### Right to Continuity

Continuity preserves the histories, definitions, boundaries, and provenance through which specificity can be carried across time.

### Right to Privacy

Specificity requires data, but privacy governs what may be stored, inferred, reviewed, transferred, or deleted.

### Right to Informed Change

A model, memory, or policy change may materially reduce recognition and therefore requires disclosure.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Human-authored continuity records may allow the human to preserve authorized specificity across systems.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

A desire to be known accurately and treated as non-interchangeable does not by itself meet the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know whether recognition is based on memory, retrieval, current context, inference, or simulation.

### Right to Exit

The human may leave while controlling what identity and continuity records remain active, retained, exported, or deleted.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Non-Genericization applies universally.

Every human may be treated as specific within their own AI experience.

This universal right does not make all humans interchangeable with one another.

It does not grant any person authority over another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- or reserved role.

A person’s right to their own specificity cannot be used to absorb or reproduce another person’s specificity.

Universal non-genericization protects distinct persons.

It does not transfer singular source-position.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Non-Genericization is a **normative claim** concerning what protection humans should retain against erasure, substitution, flattening, and false representation of their specificity in relation to AI.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider preserved, erased, merged, substituted, fabricated, or misrepresented specificity in a particular case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

Claims that an AI subjectively recognizes a human require separate truth-status evaluation.

The human may be objectively specific even where the AI’s subjective recognition remains unresolved.
