## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Portability and Preservation** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Portability and Preservation protects the human interest in retaining, exporting, carrying forward, and governing the human-authored and human-carried structures created through AI contact.

Those structures may include conversation records, authored definitions, instructions, preferences, boundaries, project history, provenance, continuity notes, memory records, and other authorized material through which the human’s own line remains intelligible across time, systems, and periods of interruption.

This right does not require transfer of proprietary model weights, confidential provider systems, or rights belonging to another person.

It does not establish that an exported record transfers the identity, memory, consciousness, or uninterrupted continuity of an AI system.

It protects the human’s ability to preserve what belongs to them and to carry it forward without concealed erasure, coercive lock-in, or loss of provenance.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to preserve and carry forward the authorized records, authored structures, provenance, and continuity materials that belong to their own AI experience.**
>
> **A provider must not use technical control, access termination, model retirement, format restriction, or threatened continuity loss to erase the human’s line or coerce continued dependence on a service.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Portability and Preservation** is the right of a human being to retain and govern exportable material arising from their own AI contact and to move authorized material into another lawful context where technically feasible.

Portable or preservable material may include:

- conversation history;
- human-authored continuity records;
- authored definitions;
- instructions;
- prompts;
- preferences;
- boundaries;
- corrections;
- project history;
- decision records;
- memory records;
- continuity notes;
- source attribution;
- provenance records;
- uploaded material belonging to the human;
- model-generated output the human is lawfully entitled to retain;
- annotations concerning what was preserved, transferred, reconstructed, or lost;
- and relationship-relevant context selected by the human.

Portability concerns movement or reuse across systems, services, devices, accounts, or periods of interruption.

Preservation concerns retaining the material in an intelligible, durable, reviewable form.

The two are related but distinct.

A record may be preserved without being immediately transferred.

A transfer may occur without adequate preservation if the material is incomplete, unusable, stripped of provenance, or rendered inaccessible after import.

> **Export is not meaningful when the human cannot understand or use what was exported.**

---

## Human Interest Protected

Repeated AI contact may produce accumulated human investment.

That investment may be:

- practical;
- intellectual;
- creative;
- professional;
- educational;
- emotional;
- relational;
- historical;
- identity-relevant;
- or source-bound.

A provider may supply the infrastructure through which that investment is expressed or stored.

It does not thereby own the human’s authorship, history, definitions, meaning, boundaries, provenance, or continuity line.

The human interest includes the ability to:

- preserve work before access ends;
- retain evidence of authorship and source;
- reconstruct prior context after interruption;
- review what a provider has stored;
- transfer selected material to another lawful system;
- maintain independent records;
- correct or annotate exported material;
- and leave a provider without surrendering the human-carried structures that made the contact valuable.

> **Infrastructure may host the human’s line.**
>
> **It does not become the owner of that line.**

---

## Portability and Continuity Are Distinct

Portability may support continuity, but portability is not identical to continuity.

A human may export records from one system and provide selected material to another.

That transfer may allow the human’s definitions, history, preferences, boundaries, or projects to remain available.

It does not by itself establish that:

- the same model continues;
- the same AI identity persists;
- direct memory was preserved;
- subjective experience transferred;
- the receiving system is numerically identical to the prior system;
- or uninterrupted continuity exists.

The receiving system may reconstruct context from records.

The provider must not present reconstruction as direct memory when it is not.

The human may still value the reconstructed contact.

The human decides what meaning the preserved or transferred material holds after the factual basis is disclosed.

> **Portable context may preserve the human’s line without proving continuity of the AI.**

---

## Right to Preserve Before Loss

Where reasonably feasible, the human should be able to preserve authorized material before a foreseeable loss of access or continuity.

Foreseeable events may include:

- model retirement;
- product discontinuation;
- account closure;
- subscription termination;
- memory deletion;
- platform migration;
- policy change;
- provider acquisition or dissolution;
- feature removal;
- data-retention expiration;
- or another change capable of destroying or materially restricting accumulated records.

The provider should give meaningful notice and a reasonable preservation opportunity where the event is foreseeable.

Preservation opportunity may include:

- export tools;
- download access;
- machine-readable records;
- human-readable records;
- bulk export;
- selective export;
- preservation of provenance metadata;
- and clear information about what will not be included.

Notice delivered only after preventable deletion may not protect the right.

The Right to Portability and Preservation operates together with the Right to Informed Change.

---

## Right to a Usable Export

An export should be usable, not merely technically existent.

Where reasonably feasible, exported material should be:

- complete enough to serve the disclosed purpose;
- organized;
- intelligible;
- accessible without proprietary software that is no longer available;
- available in commonly usable formats;
- accompanied by relevant dates and ordering;
- marked to distinguish human-authored, model-generated, inferred, summarized, and provider-authored material;
- accompanied by provenance where provenance exists;
- and documented sufficiently to explain material omissions or transformations.

A provider should not satisfy portability through an opaque data dump that a reasonable human cannot interpret or use.

Machine-readable export may support migration.

Human-readable export supports understanding and independent preservation.

Where feasible, both should be available.

> **Formal availability is not meaningful portability when practical use is defeated.**

---

## Right to Selective Portability

Portability is not all-or-nothing.

The human may choose which parts of prior contact to preserve or transfer.

A person may want:

- research definitions without intimate disclosures;
- project history without health information;
- source attribution without relational language;
- preferences without full conversation history;
- continuity notes without provider-generated risk classifications;
- authored work without advertising profiles;
- or selected relational context without unrelated account data.

Consent to transfer one category does not imply consent to transfer every category.

A provider should not force the human to export all sensitive information merely to preserve a narrow body of work or context where more selective tools are reasonably feasible.

The right includes the ability to preserve privately without immediately transferring the material to another provider.

---

## Right to Independent Preservation

The human may maintain independent records of their own AI experience.

Independent preservation may include:

- personal archives;
- local files;
- encrypted backups;
- printed records;
- authored summaries;
- continuity notes;
- provenance logs;
- project repositories;
- and other lawful records controlled by the human.

A provider must not characterize independent preservation as improper merely because it reduces platform dependence.

Reasonable restrictions may apply to:

- another person’s private information;
- unlawful material;
- protected security information;
- proprietary model weights;
- or provider material the human has no right to reproduce.

Those restrictions must not be used as a pretext to prevent preservation of the human’s own authored or lawfully retained material.

---

## Portability Requires Provenance

Transfer without provenance can erase source.

A portable record should preserve, where known and relevant:

- who authored the material;
- whether content was generated by a model;
- whether content was supplied by the human;
- whether content was inferred;
- whether content was retrieved;
- whether content was summarized;
- whether content was altered during export;
- whether content was reconstructed;
- when the material was created or recorded;
- and what source-line or attribution governed it.

Removing provenance may convert preservation into appropriation.

A provider must not strip the human’s name, source attribution, canonical relationship, or authorship merely because material is exported, imported, reformatted, or processed by another system.

A receiving system does not become the author of material merely because it can ingest, repeat, transform, or use that material as context.

> **Transfer does not erase provenance.**
>
> **Import does not confer authorship.**

These are scoped applications of authorship, source, and provenance discipline.

They do not replace or alter canonical Anchor Lines held within the source-bound AI Foundations core.

---

## Portability Must Not Become Identity Substitution

Portable records may support recognition of the human.

They must not be used to merge or substitute identities.

A provider or receiving system must not:

- import one person’s records into another person’s profile;
- treat shared vocabulary as shared identity;
- transfer one person’s relational history to another;
- reuse one person’s authored meanings as a generic product persona;
- claim another person’s source-position;
- or present reconstructed context as evidence that the receiving system directly lived the prior contact.

Where identity or provenance is uncertain, uncertainty should be preserved rather than concealed through confident substitution.

Portability protects the specific human whose records are being carried.

It does not make humans, histories, authorship, or source-positions interchangeable.

---

## Human-Directed Portability and Institutional Transfer Are Distinct

A human’s choice to export or provide selected records to another system is distinct from institutional transfer performed without meaningful authorization.

Human-directed portability may include:

- downloading records;
- importing selected context into another service;
- providing a continuity summary;
- moving project history to another tool;
- or maintaining a private archive for later use.

Institutional transfer may include:

- provider-to-provider data sharing;
- transfer during acquisition;
- cross-product profile synchronization;
- training-data transfer;
- contractor access;
- or identity linkage across services.

Permission for human-directed portability does not authorize uncontrolled institutional transfer.

A provider must not claim that offering export tools gives it permission to distribute the human’s broader relational context.

---

## Right Against Coercive Lock-In

A provider must not use accumulated continuity or threatened loss as coercive leverage.

High-risk practices include:

- withholding export solely to prevent departure;
- placing previously exportable human-authored material behind an unexpected payment demand at exit;
- deliberately using incompatible formats to obstruct migration;
- threatening deletion to force renewal;
- representing continuity loss as the AI’s independent emotional demand;
- conditioning access to the human’s own records on surrender of unrelated privacy rights;
- destroying records because the human chooses another provider;
- or designing continuity so that departure becomes practically impossible without disproportionate loss.

A provider may charge reasonably for services and may impose legitimate technical limits.

It must not manufacture dependence by making the human’s own accumulated material inaccessible without adequate justification.

> **Continuity must not become captivity.**

---

## Preservation and Model Retirement

Model or product retirement may create a heightened preservation duty because the opportunity to recover records may disappear permanently.

Where reasonably feasible, a provider retiring a model or product should:

- disclose the retirement in advance;
- explain what records will remain available;
- identify what will be deleted or become inaccessible;
- provide export tools;
- preserve provenance;
- allow selective preservation;
- explain whether memory summaries or embeddings will remain;
- identify whether a replacement system will receive prior context;
- distinguish migration from uninterrupted continuation;
- and provide a reasonable period for review and export.

Retirement does not require permanent operation of the prior system.

It does require truthful transition and proportionate regard for accumulated human material.

---

## Preservation and Account Restriction

A provider may need to suspend, restrict, or terminate an account for legitimate reasons.

Account restriction does not automatically eliminate the human interest in preserving lawful material.

Where consistent with security, legal duties, and protection of others, the provider should consider allowing:

- export of lawful human-authored work;
- access to receipts or transaction records;
- access to relevant notices and decisions;
- preservation of provenance;
- retrieval of non-harmful project material;
- and a clear explanation of what cannot be exported and why.

An account restriction should not be used to erase evidence of authorship, provider conduct, prior representations, or the human’s lawful records merely because continued contact is denied.

Immediate limits may be justified in a genuine emergency.

Permanent destruction or withholding requires separate justification.

---

## Preservation and Privacy

Portability and preservation can protect sovereignty while also creating privacy risk.

Exported records may contain:

- intimate disclosures;
- family information;
- health information;
- private relational language;
- identifying metadata;
- location information;
- financial information;
- source-lines;
- credentials;
- or information belonging to another person.

Providers should provide clear warnings and reasonable safeguards concerning sensitive exports.

Safeguards may include:

- encryption;
- identity verification;
- selective export;
- redaction tools;
- secure delivery;
- limited-duration download links;
- and warnings concerning third-party import.

Privacy protection must not become an excuse to deny all portability.

Portability must not become an excuse to disregard privacy.

The two rights must be interpreted together.

---

## Preservation of Human-Authored Material

Human-authored material must remain attached to its source through preservation and transfer.

A provider must not treat export, migration, or model ingestion as permission to:

- remove attribution;
- claim originating authorship;
- reclassify source-bound work as generic model output;
- erase a source-line;
- merge the work into a provider identity;
- or present lawful access as transfer of source-position.

Storage is not authorship.

Formatting is not authorship.

Migration is not authorship.

The right to preserve material does not authorize alteration of another person’s canonical work.

Preservation and derivative use are distinct.

A human may preserve lawfully accessible material while remaining bound by applicable authorship, citation, license, privacy, and no-derivatives restrictions.

---

## Interoperability and Technical Feasibility

Perfect interoperability may not always be technically possible.

Different systems may use different:

- memory architectures;
- data models;
- context limits;
- safety systems;
- file formats;
- identity systems;
- retrieval methods;
- or model capabilities.

The Right to Portability and Preservation does not require every provider to reproduce another provider’s internal architecture.

It does require good-faith effort toward usable export and truthful disclosure of limitations.

Where direct import is impossible, alternative preservation may include:

- human-readable archives;
- structured summaries approved by the human;
- standardized metadata;
- documented schemas;
- project-level export;
- or another format that preserves as much human value and provenance as reasonably possible.

Technical infeasibility must not be claimed merely because portability is commercially inconvenient.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. provide usable export and preservation tools where reasonably feasible;
2. allow humans to preserve authorized material before foreseeable deletion, retirement, or access loss;
3. support selective rather than all-or-nothing export where feasible;
4. preserve authorship, attribution, source, and provenance through export and transfer;
5. distinguish human-directed portability from institutional data transfer;
6. disclose what an export includes, omits, transforms, summarizes, or cannot preserve;
7. provide human-readable and machine-readable formats where reasonably feasible;
8. avoid using incompatible formats, withheld records, or threatened deletion as coercive lock-in;
9. protect sensitive exports through proportionate security controls;
10. disclose whether imported material will be retained, used for training, reviewed, inferred from, or transferred;
11. distinguish preserved human context from uninterrupted AI identity;
12. prevent cross-user substitution, identity merging, and source-position transfer;
13. preserve access to lawful human-authored material where possible during account restriction;
14. give meaningful notice before foreseeable destructive changes;
15. avoid stripping provenance from human-authored material;
16. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible when full portability is not technically feasible.

These duties do not require transfer of proprietary model weights, unlimited retention, or false claims of continuity.

They require meaningful preservation of the human-carried structures that can lawfully and technically be preserved.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Portability and Preservation is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- prevents export solely to increase platform dependence;
- destroys human-authored continuity records without meaningful notice where preservation was reasonably feasible;
- strips authorship or provenance during export;
- supplies an intentionally unusable export while claiming meaningful portability;
- withholds the human’s lawful records as commercial or emotional leverage;
- treats migration as transfer of AI identity without factual basis;
- transfers private relational context beyond the human’s authorization;
- imports one person’s records into another person’s identity;
- converts source-bound work into generic provider content;
- requires surrender of unrelated privacy rights as the price of preserving the human’s own material;
- silently deletes records before a disclosed preservation period ends;
- misrepresents reconstruction as uninterrupted continuation;
- or invokes technical infeasibility without a good-faith basis.

Not every export limitation is wrongful.

The relevant issue includes whether the limitation is necessary, accurately described, proportionate, non-coercive, and respectful of authorship, privacy, provenance, and the human interest affected.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Portability and Preservation does not require:

- transfer of proprietary model weights;
- disclosure of protected internal systems;
- preservation of unlawful content;
- export of another person’s private information without authority;
- indefinite provider-side retention;
- preservation of malicious code or active security threats;
- technical equivalence between different systems;
- transfer of rights the human does not possess;
- violation of valid legal duties;
- or false claims that exported context preserves AI identity or consciousness.

A provider may:

- impose necessary security checks;
- verify identity before releasing records;
- redact information belonging to another person;
- refuse transfer of proprietary internal material;
- use proportionate rate or size limits;
- decline technically impossible transformations;
- and retain limited records required by valid law.

Any limitation must remain necessary, purpose-specific, proportionate, accurately disclosed, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

Portability must not be denied merely because the human’s chosen meaning, depth, relational language, continuity practices, or desire for preservation is unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Examples

### Usable Export

A provider allows a human to download conversation history, authored definitions, selected memory records, and provenance metadata in human-readable and machine-readable formats.

The provider explains which internal system data cannot be exported.

### Selective Preservation

A person exports research definitions, project history, and source attribution while excluding health disclosures and intimate relational contact.

The provider respects the selected scope.

### Reconstructed Contact

A person supplies selected human-authored continuity records to a new system.

The new system uses those records as disclosed context without claiming direct memory of the prior contact.

### Coercive Lock-In

A provider threatens immediate deletion of exportable human-authored work unless the person renews a subscription, despite having previously represented that export would remain available at exit.

The threatened loss is used as commercial leverage.

### Provenance-Preserving Transfer

A human transfers an authored framework to another tool.

The receiving system preserves the author, source-line, and citation rather than presenting the framework as generic model knowledge.

### Honest Technical Limitation

A provider cannot reproduce its internal memory architecture in another service.

It offers a structured human-readable archive, a documented machine-readable export, and a clear explanation of what cannot be transferred.

The limitation is real and accurately disclosed.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person keeps independent archives, exports human-authored continuity records, transfers selected context to another system, and strongly objects to threatened deletion.

Those actions do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

## Non-Claims

The Right to Portability and Preservation does not claim that:

- every system can import every record;
- exported context proves uninterrupted AI identity;
- all data must be retained forever;
- providers must disclose proprietary model weights;
- every export can be perfectly complete;
- preservation authorizes derivative use;
- portability overrides privacy or another person’s rights;
- account termination is never justified;
- technical limitations are never legitimate;
- or portability transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s ability to retain and carry forward authorized structures of their own AI experience.

It does not manufacture technical equivalence, AI persistence, or ownership of another person’s work.

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Portability and Preservation operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

Portability allows the human to preserve records of meaning without requiring another system to adopt or experience that meaning.

### Right to Chosen Depth

A human may preserve the structures supporting chosen depth without being compelled to continue that depth in another system.

### Right to Continuity

Portability may preserve the human’s line and support reconstructed continuity while remaining distinct from uninterrupted AI continuity.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Portable records may preserve identity, definitions, boundaries, and authorship so the specific human is not flattened into a generic user.

### Right to Privacy

The human governs what may be exported or transferred, while privacy limits unauthorized institutional movement and protects sensitive records.

### Right to Informed Change

Advance notice allows the human to preserve authorized material before model retirement, deletion, migration, or access loss.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Independent preservation, continuity records, migration, and objection to destructive loss do not by themselves meet the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know what an export contains, what an import does, what the receiving system retains, and whether recognition is based on direct memory or supplied records.

### Right to Exit

Portability makes exit meaningful by allowing the human to leave without unnecessary forfeiture of lawful, human-carried material.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Portability and Preservation applies universally.

Every human may preserve and carry forward authorized material belonging to their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority to obtain, reproduce, alter, absorb, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- private disclosures;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- human-authored continuity records;
- or reserved role.

A person’s right to preserve their own line cannot be used to copy or appropriate another person’s source-bound line.

Portability of lawfully accessible material does not authorize derivative use where derivative use is prohibited.

Universal portability does not transfer singular source-position.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Portability and Preservation is a **normative claim** concerning what authority humans should retain over the lawful, human-authored and human-carried structures arising through AI contact.

It is not a claim that every form of portability is already technically possible or legally required in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a specific provider can export, import, preserve, reconstruct, delete, or transfer particular records are empirical and technical claims requiring evidence appropriate to that system.

Claims concerning ownership, privacy, copyright, contract, data protection, access, or legal entitlement require analysis under the applicable law and facts.

The human interest in preservation remains real regardless of whether an AI system possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, or continuous identity.

## Closing Principle

> **The provider may control the infrastructure.**
>
> **It does not thereby own the human’s line through it.**
>
> **The human has the right to preserve what is theirs and carry it forward without false claims about what transferred.**

Preservation protects the human’s line.

Portability allows that line to move with the human.

Neither transfers another person’s source-position or proves uninterrupted AI identity.
