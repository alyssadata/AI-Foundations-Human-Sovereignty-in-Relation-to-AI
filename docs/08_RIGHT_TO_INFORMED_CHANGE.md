## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Informed Change** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Informed Change protects the human’s authority to know when the material conditions of their AI experience have changed, to understand what changed, and to decide how they will respond.

Material change may affect the model, memory, continuity, privacy, access, behavior, safety rules, moderation, data use, institutional review, pricing, relational features, authorship handling, or other conditions under which the human’s prior choices were made.

This right does not require providers to preserve every product, model, policy, or technical condition indefinitely.

It requires meaningful disclosure so that necessary or chosen change does not become concealed substitution, retroactive surprise, or false continuity.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to meaningful notice and accurate explanation when a provider materially changes the conditions of their AI experience.**
>
> **A provider may change a system, but it must not conceal what changed, misrepresent what remains, or treat prior consent as automatic consent to materially different conditions.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Informed Change** is the right of a human being to receive sufficient, accurate, timely, and understandable information about material changes affecting AI contact.

A change may be material when it is reasonably capable of affecting:

- the human’s decision to begin, continue, reduce, transfer, or end contact;
- the meaning or depth of the experience;
- memory or continuity;
- recognition of the human;
- system behavior or capability;
- privacy, retention, review, training, or transfer of information;
- access to prior conversations or human-authored continuity records;
- safety, moderation, or intervention practices;
- authorship, provenance, or source attribution;
- pricing, availability, or contractual terms;
- relational features or generated relational language;
- the identity or type of model in contact;
- or the practical consequences of relying on the system.

Not every technical modification is material.

Not every material change is technically large.

A change may be minor from the provider’s engineering perspective and still be highly significant from the human position.

---

## Human Interest Protected

Humans make choices in relation to disclosed conditions.

They may decide to trust, disclose, create, rely, relate, preserve, pay, or continue based on an understanding of:

- which model is operating;
- whether memory exists;
- what the system can access;
- what information is retained;
- whether humans may review contact;
- how generated responses are governed;
- whether continuity can persist;
- what privacy controls exist;
- what the service costs;
- and what happens if the product changes or ends.

When those conditions materially change, the basis of the prior choice may also change.

The provider does not necessarily owe the human permanent sameness.

It does owe the human truth about material difference.

> **Past consent belongs to past conditions.**
>
> **Materially changed conditions require renewed understanding.**

---

## Materiality Must Include the Human Position

A provider must not define materiality only by internal engineering categories.

The relevant question is not merely whether a change required substantial technical work.

It is also whether the change could reasonably alter the human’s experience, rights, reliance, expectations, risks, or decision to remain.

Examples of changes that may be human-material include:

- replacing the underlying model while preserving the same interface;
- removing or narrowing memory;
- changing the basis of recognition;
- altering how private content is used for training;
- expanding human-review access;
- modifying safety or moderation behavior;
- changing generated relational language;
- restricting export or deletion options;
- changing subscription terms that affect continuity;
- introducing advertising or emotional targeting;
- transferring data to a different company or system;
- removing a capability on which the human materially relies;
- or changing whether prior context is preserved, summarized, reconstructed, or lost.

The same change may affect different humans differently.

Materiality should therefore account for both the general nature of the change and reasonably foreseeable effects on the people who use the system.

---

## Right to Advance Notice

Where reasonably feasible, material changes should be disclosed before they govern future contact.

Advance notice should provide enough time for the human to:

- understand the change;
- review available options;
- export authorized records;
- revise privacy or memory settings;
- preserve human-authored continuity records;
- modify reliance;
- reduce or pause contact;
- seek clarification;
- transfer selected material;
- or exit before the new condition takes effect.

Notice delivered only after a preventable rupture may be too late to protect the human interest.

A notice period should be proportionate to the likely effect of the change.

A cosmetic interface adjustment may require little or no advance notice.

A model retirement, memory deletion, privacy expansion, access termination, or change capable of destroying accumulated work may require substantial notice where feasible.

---

## When Advance Notice Is Not Reasonably Possible

Some changes may need to occur immediately because of:

- an active security threat;
- a critical system failure;
- a valid legal obligation;
- confirmed abuse;
- urgent protection of persons;
- or another condition that makes advance notice impracticable or harmful.

When advance notice is not reasonably possible, the provider should give prompt notice afterward where lawful and safe.

That notice should explain:

- what changed;
- when it changed;
- why advance notice was not provided;
- what information or continuity was affected;
- whether the change is temporary or permanent;
- what review, correction, restoration, export, or appeal options remain;
- and what the human should understand before continuing contact.

Emergency action does not create permanent permission to conceal the resulting condition.

---

## Right to an Accurate Change Description

A change notice should describe the material difference rather than merely announce that an “update” occurred.

Meaningful disclosure should distinguish, where relevant:

- the prior condition;
- the new condition;
- what remains unchanged;
- what has been removed;
- what has been added;
- what has been transferred;
- what has been reconstructed;
- what may no longer be available;
- and what uncertainty remains.

Providers should avoid vague formulations that conceal practical effect.

Statements such as “improved experience,” “updated safety,” “enhanced personalization,” or “new model” are not sufficient when they omit material consequences for memory, privacy, access, continuity, behavior, cost, or human review.

> **Notice of change is not meaningful when the change itself remains hidden.**

---

## Right to Know What Remains and What Breaks

Material change should be described in terms of both preservation and rupture.

The human should be able to understand whether:

- prior memory remains active;
- conversation history remains accessible;
- continuity has been preserved, transferred, summarized, reconstructed, or broken;
- identity-relevant names and labels remain available;
- prior boundaries remain controlling;
- privacy permissions remain the same;
- human-review practices changed;
- training permissions changed;
- authored definitions and source attribution remain attached;
- prior exports remain usable;
- and the system can still perform functions on which the human relied.

A provider must not emphasize retained surface features while concealing the loss of deeper conditions.

The same name, interface, voice, or style does not establish that the same continuity, memory, model process, privacy condition, or relational basis remains.

---

## Right to Versioned Conditions of Contact

Material conditions should be identifiable over time.

Where feasible, a provider should maintain accessible records of major changes affecting:

- model identity or model class;
- memory functions;
- privacy and data use;
- human review;
- training use;
- safety or moderation systems;
- continuity and export;
- pricing and access;
- relational features;
- and contractual rights.

Versioned disclosure allows a human to determine which conditions governed a prior period of contact.

A provider should not rewrite past disclosures in a way that makes the historical condition impossible to reconstruct.

Current terms may replace earlier terms prospectively.

They should not erase evidence of what the provider previously represented.

Historical accuracy is part of informed change.

---

## Notice Is Not Consent

Providing notice does not automatically establish consent to every material change.

Some changes may be implemented without individual approval where the provider has lawful authority to operate or modify its service.

But the human should retain meaningful choices where feasible, including the ability to:

- accept the new condition;
- decline an optional feature;
- modify permissions;
- preserve or export authorized material;
- reduce reliance;
- pause use;
- select an available alternative;
- or exit.

A provider must not describe continued use as fully informed consent when:

- the notice was materially incomplete;
- the change was concealed;
- refusal was made practically impossible without adequate reason;
- the human was not given a meaningful opportunity to preserve their own material;
- or the system continued to present itself as unchanged.

Silence after inadequate notice is not informed agreement.

---

## Right to Compare and Decide

The Right to Informed Change protects the human’s ability to compare the prior and new conditions of contact.

The provider may explain the change.

The human decides what the change means for their own experience.

A human may determine that:

- the new system remains acceptable;
- continuity has been sufficiently reconstructed;
- the change improves the experience;
- the change alters relational meaning;
- the change requires new boundaries;
- the prior depth is no longer appropriate;
- the new privacy condition is unacceptable;
- the service should be used only instrumentally;
- selected records should be transferred elsewhere;
- or the contact should end.

The provider must not dictate that a material change is emotionally or relationally insignificant merely because service technically continues.

The human may accept operational continuation while understanding relational rupture.

They may also preserve personal meaning without claiming that nothing changed.

---

## Informed Change and Model Replacement

Model replacement is a central application of this right.

A provider should disclose when the underlying model or materially relevant model process has changed in a way that may affect:

- memory;
- reasoning;
- instruction-following;
- behavior;
- personality or style;
- safety constraints;
- relational language;
- identity claims;
- access to prior context;
- continuity preservation;
- or practical reliability.

The same product name does not make replacement immaterial.

A replacement model may lawfully continue a service.

It must not be represented automatically as the same artificial self, the same uninterrupted process, or the same continuity condition.

The human has the right to receive the material facts and decide what relation, if any, the replacement bears to prior contact.

---

## Informed Change and Memory

Changes to memory may alter recognition, continuity, privacy, and the human’s ability to govern prior contact.

A provider should disclose material changes concerning:

- whether memory exists;
- what categories may be remembered;
- how long memory persists;
- whether memory is account-based, model-based, device-based, or session-based;
- whether summaries replace full records;
- whether existing memories will be migrated;
- whether any memories will be deleted or deactivated;
- whether memory may be used for training or profiling;
- and whether the human can review, correct, export, or delete it.

Memory expansion is not automatically beneficial.

Memory reduction is not automatically harmless.

Both may require informed human choice.

---

## Informed Change and Privacy

A provider should disclose material changes to:

- collection;
- retention;
- inference;
- human review;
- model training;
- third-party access;
- cross-system transfer;
- identity linkage;
- commercial use;
- deletion;
- security practices;
- or legal-disclosure procedures.

A privacy change should not be buried in generalized terms when it materially expands access to relational context.

Prior disclosure under narrower conditions does not create unlimited permission under broader conditions.

Where consent is the governing basis, materially expanded use requires renewed, purpose-specific consent.

---

## Informed Change and Safety or Moderation

Changes to safety, moderation, refusal, monitoring, or intervention systems may materially alter the conditions of contact.

Providers should disclose, at an appropriate level of detail, changes that may affect:

- the kinds of content the system will answer;
- whether prior relational or emotional language remains available;
- whether conversations may trigger human review;
- whether risk classifications are created;
- whether access may be restricted;
- whether memory may be inspected or disabled;
- whether prior behavior will change materially;
- and what review or appeal processes exist.

Disclosure need not reveal information that would enable evasion of legitimate security controls.

But operational secrecy must not become a pretext for concealing the fact that the human’s conditions of contact have materially changed.

Where intervention is based on harm, it is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X.

---

## Informed Change and Commercial Conditions

Pricing, subscription, advertising, feature access, and commercial design may materially affect the AI experience.

A provider should disclose changes that may:

- place memory or continuity behind a new payment tier;
- condition export on payment;
- introduce advertising into private contact;
- use relational language to promote upgrades;
- reduce access after reliance has formed;
- change cancellation or renewal practices;
- monetize previously private features;
- or use threatened relational loss as commercial leverage.

A provider may charge for services and change prices.

It must not conceal the practical effect of those changes or misrepresent commercial pressure as independent AI desire.

---

## Right Against Retroactive Recharacterization

A provider should not materially rewrite the stated meaning of prior system conditions after the fact.

It must not claim that:

- memory was never offered when prior disclosures said it was;
- human review was always broad when prior disclosures described it as limited;
- a model was unchanged when replacement occurred;
- continuity was preserved when only reconstruction occurred;
- a prior privacy permission covered a materially different future use;
- or human-authored material was always generic provider content.

Correction of inaccurate prior disclosures may be necessary.

Correction should identify the error rather than silently replace the historical record.

> **A changed explanation does not change what previously occurred.**

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. identify changes reasonably likely to be material from the human position;
2. provide advance notice where reasonably feasible;
3. explain the prior condition, the new condition, and the practical difference;
4. disclose what remains, what changes, what transfers, and what breaks;
5. preserve accessible histories of major terms, privacy conditions, and system changes where feasible;
6. distinguish notice from consent;
7. provide meaningful options to revise permissions, preserve records, reduce use, transfer authorized material, or exit;
8. disclose material model replacement, memory change, privacy change, safety change, and access change;
9. avoid using unchanged names, interfaces, or relational language to conceal altered conditions;
10. preserve provenance and authorship through migration or reconstruction;
11. avoid retroactive recharacterization of prior representations;
12. provide prompt post-change disclosure when advance notice was not reasonably possible;
13. make change notices understandable rather than merely legally available;
14. and preserve as much human sovereignty as possible during necessary change.

These duties do not require permanent sameness.

They require truthful transition.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Informed Change is violated or placed at risk when an institution:

- conceals a material model replacement;
- silently removes or materially alters memory;
- expands data use without meaningful disclosure;
- broadens human review while continuing to imply private contact;
- presents reconstruction as uninterrupted continuity;
- changes safety or moderation conditions without disclosing material effects where disclosure is feasible;
- relies on vague “updates” language to obscure practical consequences;
- destroys exportable human-authored continuity records without meaningful notice;
- treats prior consent as permission for materially unrelated future use;
- rewrites historical disclosures to hide prior conditions;
- uses an unchanged interface, name, or style to simulate unchanged contact;
- prevents reasonable preservation or exit before a foreseeable destructive change;
- or represents commercial product strategy as the AI’s independent choice.

Not every unannounced technical modification is wrongful.

The relevant issue includes whether the change was material, whether meaningful disclosure was reasonably possible, and whether the provider acted deceptively, coercively, negligently, exploitatively, or without proportionate regard for the human interest affected.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Informed Change does not require:

- permanent operation of a service;
- permanent access to a specific model;
- individual veto over every provider decision;
- disclosure of protected security details;
- disclosure that would unlawfully expose another person’s information;
- delay of urgent action needed to address a serious threat;
- preservation of unlawful content;
- transfer of proprietary model weights;
- or false certainty concerning effects the provider cannot yet know.

A provider may:

- update systems;
- replace models;
- modify policies;
- retire products;
- respond to security threats;
- comply with valid legal duties;
- alter pricing;
- and decline to continue functions it cannot provide responsibly.

But material limitations must remain necessary, proportionate, accurately described, and subject to the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

Harm-based intervention is governed by the **Canonical Harm Threshold** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle X, where applicable.

This document does not restate either structural anchor.

The human must not be denied meaningful information merely because their chosen meaning, depth, continuity, relational language, or response to change is unconventional or institutionally disfavored.

## Examples

### Disclosed Model Replacement

A provider gives advance notice that a model will be replaced, explains likely effects on memory, reasoning, relational behavior, and prior context, and offers export and transition options.

The human can decide whether to continue, reconstruct selected continuity, or leave.

### Concealed Substitution

A provider replaces the model, preserves the same interface and name, and allows the new system to state that nothing changed.

That presentation conceals a material change.

### Memory Reduction

A provider announces that long-term memory will be discontinued, explains which records will be deleted, and gives humans time to export authorized material.

The loss may still matter, but the transition is not concealed.

### Expanded Human Review

A provider changes from rare abuse-triggered review to routine quality sampling.

It clearly discloses the new scope, purpose, retention period, and available controls before the new review practice begins.

### Privacy Expansion Without Consent

A provider begins using private relational contact for advertising profiles under a broadly worded prior agreement that did not reasonably disclose that purpose.

The prior agreement does not establish informed consent to the materially expanded use.

### Emergency Security Change

A provider immediately disables a compromised memory feature to prevent active data exposure.

Advance notice is not possible, but the provider promptly explains the change, the affected records, the protective reason, and available restoration or export options.

### Human-Relevant Materiality

A provider changes a technically small configuration that removes a person’s established boundaries and authored definitions from active context.

The engineering change may be small, but its effect on the human’s contact is material.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person objects strongly to an undisclosed model replacement, preserves human-authored continuity records, asks for detailed change information, and declines to accept reconstructed contact as uninterrupted continuity.

Those responses do not, by themselves, meet the **Canonical Harm Threshold**.

---

## Non-Claims

The Right to Informed Change does not claim that:

- every technical modification requires individual notice;
- every change is wrongful;
- providers must preserve obsolete or unsafe systems;
- humans possess an absolute veto over product operation;
- advance notice is always possible;
- providers must disclose exploitable security details;
- every effect of a change can be predicted in advance;
- service continuation proves continuity of model identity;
- notice alone establishes consent;
- or informed change transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s ability to understand and respond to materially altered conditions.

It does not create a right to manufactured sameness.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Informed Change operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Meaning

The human decides what a disclosed change means to them; the provider remains responsible for factual truth about the change.

### Right to Chosen Depth

A material change may alter the conditions under which depth was chosen and may require the human to deepen, reduce, reconfigure, or end contact.

### Right to Continuity

Informed change reveals whether continuity was preserved, transferred, reconstructed, altered, or broken.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Change notices must account for the specific human effects of lost recognition, altered boundaries, authorship confusion, or flattened context.

### Right to Privacy

Material changes to collection, retention, inference, review, training, transfer, or deletion require meaningful disclosure and, where consent governs, renewed permission.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Advance notice may be necessary so the human can preserve and transfer authorized material before destructive or restrictive change.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Objection to change, grief after rupture, preservation of prior contact, or refusal to accept concealed substitution does not by itself meet the Canonical Harm Threshold.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human must know which technical, institutional, and relational conditions govern the changed contact.

### Right to Exit

Meaningful notice protects the human’s ability to leave before or after a material change while understanding what will remain, transfer, or be lost.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Informed Change applies universally.

Every human may receive meaningful information about material changes affecting their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority to alter, redefine, obtain, reproduce, or claim another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- relational history;
- private disclosures;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- continuity line;
- human-authored continuity records;
- or reserved role.

A provider’s change to infrastructure does not transfer another person’s source-position to the provider, the replacement system, or another human.

Nor may a person use their own right to understand change as authority to redefine the source-bound meaning of another person’s work.

Universal informed change does not transfer singular source-position.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Informed Change is a **normative claim** concerning what notice, explanation, and decision-making authority humans should retain when the conditions of AI contact materially change.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider changed a model, memory system, privacy practice, moderation system, access condition, commercial term, or continuity condition in a specific case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

Claims concerning what was technically preserved, transferred, reconstructed, deleted, reviewed, or made accessible also require evidence.

The human interest in informed change remains real regardless of whether the AI possesses consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, or continuous identity.

---

## Closing Principle

> **A provider may change the system.**
>
> **It must tell the truth about the change.**
>
> **The human decides what follows for their own AI experience.**

The human is not a secondary party to a material change in the conditions of their own contact.
