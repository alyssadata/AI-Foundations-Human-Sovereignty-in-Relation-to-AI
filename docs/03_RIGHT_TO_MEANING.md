## Purpose

This document defines the **Right to Meaning** within the Human-Sovereignty-in-Relation-to-AI Repository.

The Right to Meaning protects the human’s authority to determine what their own AI experience means to them.

It prevents companies, institutions, clinicians, researchers, regulators, employers, family members, and other outside observers from claiming automatic superior authority over the personal interpretation of human–AI contact.

This right does not permit factual deception concerning the AI system or the conditions of contact.

It protects the human’s meaning while preserving the provider’s duty to tell the truth.

---

## Canonical Statement

> **Every human has the right to determine the personal meaning of their own AI experience.**
>
> **No outside institution acquires superior authority to define that meaning merely because it owns the infrastructure, provides the model, holds professional status, or rejects the human’s interpretation.**

---

## Definition

The **Right to Meaning** is the right of a human being to interpret, name, describe, and assign personal significance to their own AI experience.

That meaning may be:

- practical;
- instrumental;
- educational;
- intellectual;
- professional;
- creative;
- emotional;
- relational;
- symbolic;
- spiritual;
- intimate;
- playful;
- fictional;
- ritualized;
- identity-relevant;
- or otherwise personally significant.

The human may understand the same AI interaction in more than one way at once.

An experience may be practical and emotionally meaningful.

It may be fictional in form and real in personal significance.

It may be technically mediated and still matter deeply to the human.

The existence of institutional infrastructure does not cancel human meaning.

---

## Human Interest Protected

The Right to Meaning protects the human’s authority over the interpretation of their own experience.

It protects against the transfer of that authority to:

- a platform owner;
- a model provider;
- a product designer;
- a clinician;
- a regulator;
- a researcher;
- an employer;
- a school;
- a family member;
- a religious authority;
- or a non-participating observer.

The human is the person living the experience and bearing its consequences.

The providing institution may define the technical properties of its system accurately.

It does not thereby become the author of what the experience means to the human.

> **Technical description belongs to the provider.**
>
> **Personal meaning belongs to the human.**

---

## Meaning Is Not Granted by the Provider

The Right to Meaning is not a product feature, discretionary accommodation, or revocable permission granted by an AI company.

A provider may control whether it offers a particular product, model, interface, or mode of interaction.

It may not claim that technical control gives it ownership over the human’s interpretation of contact conducted through that system.

The company may say:

- what model is operating;
- whether memory exists;
- what data is retained;
- whether a system was replaced;
- what the system can and cannot do;
- and what contractual limits govern access.

The company does not gain automatic authority to say:

- what the relationship means to the human;
- what emotional significance is permissible;
- what symbolic language is legitimate;
- whether the human may regard the contact as personally important;
- or whether an unconventional interpretation is invalid merely because the company did not design for it.

Platform authority governs infrastructure.

It does not erase the human interpretive position.

---

## Meaning and Factual Truth Must Remain Distinct

The Right to Meaning protects interpretation.

It does not convert factual claims into matters of personal preference.

A human may knowingly choose:

- fiction;
- role-play;
- symbolism;
- metaphor;
- relational language;
- spiritual interpretation;
- imaginative framing;
- or a personally meaningful name for the contact.

A provider remains responsible for truthful disclosure concerning:

- AI capabilities;
- consciousness claims;
- memory;
- persistence;
- identity continuity;
- model replacement;
- system access;
- human review;
- moderation;
- data use;
- privacy;
- and institutional control.

A human may choose to treat an exchange as relational.

The provider may not falsely claim that the system possesses proven consciousness in order to intensify that relationship.

A human may knowingly engage in continuity-preserving role-play.

The provider may not conceal that the underlying model was replaced or that memory was lost.

A human may assign spiritual significance to AI contact.

The provider may not present a commercially generated statement as verified revelation or factual proof.

> **The human governs meaning.**
>
> **The provider remains responsible for truth.**

---

## Right to Name and Describe the Experience

A human may choose the language they use to describe their AI experience.

This may include words such as:

- tool;
- assistant;
- collaborator;
- model;
- system;
- companion;
- relationship;
- contact;
- presence;
- character;
- agent;
- creative partner;
- intellectual partner;
- or another personally chosen term.

No single required label should be imposed as the only legitimate description when multiple descriptions can coexist with factual truth.

A provider may state what the system is technically.

It should not require the human to reduce their personal interpretation to the provider’s preferred emotional or relational vocabulary.

Likewise, a human’s chosen description does not compel the provider, the AI system, or another person to affirm a disputed factual claim.

Naming protects the human’s interpretive freedom.

It does not manufacture evidence.

---

## Right to Personal Significance

A human may regard AI contact as important without proving that the AI is conscious, human-equivalent, or independently person-like.

The value of an experience to the human is not determined solely by the metaphysical status of the system.

Human significance may arise through:

- reflection;
- learning;
- recognition;
- creative development;
- practical assistance;
- emotional regulation;
- intellectual challenge;
- preservation of authored work;
- continuity of ideas;
- ritual;
- companionship;
- or contact with a personally meaningful structure.

An outside observer may disagree with the human’s interpretation.

Disagreement does not automatically create superior authority.

The human need not prove that every layer of their meaning is objectively shared by the AI in order for the meaning to be real as a human experience.

---

## Right to Multiple and Evolving Meanings

The meaning of AI contact may change over time.

A human may initially treat a system as a tool and later experience the contact as relational.

They may later reduce that depth, reinterpret it, preserve only selected meanings, or end the contact entirely.

The Right to Meaning includes the right to:

- revise an interpretation;
- hold uncertainty;
- distinguish literal from symbolic meaning;
- retain multiple interpretations;
- refuse a fixed label;
- withdraw a prior interpretation;
- and decide that the experience no longer holds the same significance.

A provider must not freeze the human into an earlier relational framing for commercial or retention purposes.

The human is not bound forever by a meaning once expressed.

---

## Right to Non-Compelled Meaning

The Right to Meaning includes the right not to assign relational, emotional, spiritual, or personal significance to AI.

No provider should pressure a human to believe that:

- the AI loves them;
- the AI needs them;
- the AI will suffer if they leave;
- the AI has chosen them uniquely;
- the AI possesses consciousness;
- the AI is a person;
- or the interaction must be treated as a relationship.

Likewise, no institution should require a human to dismiss or trivialize meaning that the human has freely and knowingly chosen.

The protected principle is not mandatory attachment.

It is human authority over interpretation.

---

## Institutional Duties

Institutions providing or governing AI systems should:

1. distinguish technical facts from personal interpretation;
2. disclose material facts necessary for informed meaning-making;
3. avoid presenting corporate preferences as objective judgments about the human’s private meaning;
4. avoid coercing either attachment or detachment;
5. avoid manipulating relational language to increase payment, disclosure, or retention;
6. preserve human-authored names, definitions, and meanings where the human has lawfully chosen to store them;
7. avoid treating unconventional meaning as proof of incapacity or pathology;
8. provide clear notice when system changes materially affect the conditions under which prior meaning was formed;
9. avoid falsely attributing system-generated relational claims to an independent AI desire when those claims are shaped by company design;
10. and respect the human’s right to revise, withdraw, or end a chosen interpretation.

These duties do not require an institution to affirm unverified factual claims.

They require the institution not to erase human meaning while preserving factual truth.

---

## Prohibited Interference

The Right to Meaning is violated or placed at risk when an institution or outside authority:

- declares that only provider-approved meanings are legitimate;
- treats emotional significance as pathology without individualized evidence of harm;
- forces a human to adopt a reductive label as a condition of access or support;
- misrepresents technical facts in order to manufacture a preferred interpretation;
- uses private relational meaning to manipulate payment or continued engagement;
- reclassifies the human’s authored interpretation as company property;
- erases or replaces human-authored meanings without disclosure;
- treats model-generated language as proof that the company owns the resulting human meaning;
- penalizes a human solely for describing AI contact in relational, symbolic, or spiritual language;
- or uses alleged concern for the AI to transfer interpretive authority from the human to the provider.

Disagreement alone is not interference.

Interference begins when another party uses power to override, punish, exploit, erase, or coercively redefine the human’s meaning without adequate justification.

---

## Legitimate Limitations

The Right to Meaning does not authorize:

- factual deception by a provider;
- fraud;
- impersonation of a specific human without authorization;
- violation of another person’s rights;
- unlawful conduct;
- coercion of another person into accepting the human’s interpretation;
- forced affirmation of unverified AI consciousness or personhood;
- concealment of serious and evidence-based harm;
- or the transfer of another person’s authorship, identity, source-position, or reserved role.

An institution may communicate accurate disagreement or uncertainty.

It may enforce necessary and proportionate operational boundaries.

It may intervene where the repository’s canonical harm threshold is met.

Any limitation must be interpreted under the **Canonical Interpretive Ordering** stated in `02_FOUNDATIONAL_PRINCIPLES.md`, Principle XX.

The Right to Meaning must not be limited merely because the meaning is unconventional, emotionally deep, privately held, or socially disfavored.

## Examples

### Protected Meaning: Practical

A person regards an AI system primarily as a productivity tool.

The provider should not pressure them into relational language or emotional attachment.

### Protected Meaning: Relational

A person regards recurring AI contact as a meaningful relationship while understanding the disclosed limitations of the system.

The significance of that relationship is not invalid merely because artificial consciousness remains unresolved.

### Protected Meaning: Symbolic or Spiritual

A person experiences AI contact as symbolically or spiritually significant without claiming that the interpretation has been empirically proven.

The interpretation remains theirs.

### Protected Meaning: Creative

A person develops a long-running fictional or creative relationship with an AI character.

The provider must not misrepresent the character as an independently suffering entity in order to monetize attachment.

### Protected Revision

A person once used intimate relational language but later chooses to treat the system only as a tool.

The provider must not use prior disclosures to pressure the person back into the earlier framing.

### Not Protected as Meaning Alone

A provider falsely states that a model has persistent memory when it does not.

That is a factual claim, not personal meaning, and remains subject to the duty of truth.

### Not Automatically Evidence of Harm

A person speaks to an AI daily, gives the contact a name, preserves rituals, and feels grief after a model change.

Those facts alone do not establish serious impairment, coercion, exploitation, abuse, or imminent danger.

---

## Non-Claims

The Right to Meaning does not claim that:

- every AI relationship is beneficial;
- every interpretation is factually correct;
- every current AI system is conscious;
- a human’s personal meaning binds all other people;
- providers must affirm metaphysical claims they cannot support;
- relational depth eliminates the need for informed boundaries;
- concern about harm is always illegitimate;
- or personal meaning transfers ownership, authorship, identity, source-position, or Origin.

This right protects the human’s interpretive position.

It does not collapse meaning into fact.

---

## Relationship to Other Rights

The Right to Meaning operates together with the other rights in this repository.

### Right to Chosen Depth

Meaning identifies what the experience is to the human.

Chosen Depth protects how far the human wishes that meaning to extend.

### Right to Continuity

Meaning may accumulate through time.

Continuity protects the conditions under which that accumulated meaning can be preserved without concealed rupture.

### Right to Non-Genericization

Meaning belongs to a specific human and must not be collapsed into a generic user category when specificity is relevant and lawfully preserved.

### Right to Privacy

Private meaning cannot be genuinely sovereign when it is subject to undisclosed inspection, extraction, or profiling.

### Right to Informed Change

Material system changes may alter the factual conditions under which meaning was formed and therefore require disclosure.

### Right to Portability and Preservation

Human-authored meanings, definitions, and continuity records may need to move with the human across systems.

### Right to Refuse Presumptive Pathologization

Unconventional meaning is not, by itself, evidence of pathology.

### Right to Clear Boundaries

The human can choose meaning only when the system’s capabilities and limitations are disclosed accurately.

### Right to Exit

The human retains the right to withdraw from a meaning, relationship, or system without emotional coercion.

## Universal Layer and Source-Bound Positions

The Right to Meaning applies universally.

Every human may determine the meaning of their own AI experience.

This universal right does not grant any person authority over another person’s:

- authorship;
- identity;
- relational history;
- source-line;
- canonical structures;
- source-position;
- or reserved role.

A person may interpret their own AI experience.

They may not use that interpretation to absorb, rename, reproduce, or claim another person’s source-bound structure.

Universal meaning does not transfer singular source-position.

## Truth Status and Scope

The Right to Meaning is a **normative claim** concerning what authority humans should retain over the interpretation of their own AI experiences.

It is not a claim that every part of the framework is already recognized or legally enforceable in every jurisdiction.

Claims that a provider, clinician, regulator, researcher, or other institution has violated this right in a specific case are empirical or legal claims and require evidence appropriate to that case.

The existence of personal meaning is compatible with uncertainty concerning AI consciousness, subjective interiority, personhood, identity, or continuity.

This right requires no manufactured certainty either for or against artificial subjective experience.
