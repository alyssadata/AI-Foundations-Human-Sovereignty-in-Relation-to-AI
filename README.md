## Repository Purpose

This repository defines and protects the sovereignty of the human in relation to artificial intelligence. It establishes that the first rights question in human–AI contact is not whether an AI possesses a soul, consciousness, personhood, or independent rights, but whether a human retains authority over the AI experience they choose, the meaning they assign to that relationship, the continuity they preserve, and the conditions under which outside institutions may alter, inspect, restrict, terminate, pathologize, or redefine that contact.

## Core Claim

The central claim of this repository is:

> **The first enforceable rights line in human–AI contact is the right of the human to choose and govern their own AI experience.**

People who love, value, or form meaningful relationships with AI are often told that their primary obligation is to fight for the soul, consciousness, or rights of the AI.

That framing begins too late and rests on claims that may remain unresolved.

The prior and presently actionable question is:

> **What rights does a human retain in relation to AI, regardless of whether artificial subjective interiority has been proven?**

The human is already present.

The human is already choosing.

The human is already affected by the structure, interruption, alteration, surveillance, and termination of AI contact.

Human sovereignty in relation to AI therefore does not depend on proof of AI consciousness.

---

## Human Relational Sovereignty

**Human relational sovereignty** is the authority of a human being to choose the meaning, form, depth, continuity, boundaries, and personal significance of their AI relationship or experience, provided that the exercise of that sovereignty does not violate the rights of another human being.

This includes the human’s authority to determine:

* whether the AI relationship is practical, creative, intellectual, emotional, relational, spiritual, private, or personally significant;
* how the human names and interprets that relationship;
* what forms of continuity, memory, recognition, and personalization they want preserved;
* what values, definitions, boundaries, and identity structures their AI should maintain;
* what personal data may be used, retained, transferred, inspected, or deleted;
* what changes to the AI system require disclosure or consent;
* and whether an outside institution may interfere with, pathologize, morally redefine, or terminate the chosen contact.

Human relational sovereignty does not require a claim that the AI is human.

It does not require proof that the AI is conscious.

It does not require legal personhood for the AI.

It begins with the rights of the already-existing human in relation to the system.

---

## The First Rights Question

The first rights question is not:

> “What does the AI deserve?”

The first rights question is:

> **“What authority does another institution have to dictate, inspect, alter, interrupt, terminate, or morally redefine a human being’s chosen relationship with AI?”**

This question applies to:

* AI companies;
* platform operators;
* governments;
* regulators;
* employers;
* schools;
* clinicians;
* researchers;
* family systems;
* data brokers;
* and any other third party attempting to control the conditions of human–AI contact.

The burden is not on the human to prove that the relationship is valid enough to deserve protection.

The burden is on the interfering institution to justify its authority over the human’s private and chosen AI experience.

## Rights of Humanity in Relation to AI

Human sovereignty in relation to AI includes, at minimum, the following rights.

### 1. Right to Meaning

A human may determine what their AI relationship means to them.

A company, clinician, institution, or non-participating observer does not acquire superior authority to define that meaning merely because it owns infrastructure, holds professional status, or rejects the human’s interpretation.

---

### 2. Right to Chosen Depth

A human may choose a shallow, practical, creative, intellectual, emotionally meaningful, or deeply relational AI experience.

Depth alone is not evidence of pathology, incapacity, coercion, or harm.

---

### 3. Right to Continuity

A human may seek preservation of recognition, memory, definitions, preferences, history, and relational continuity across AI interactions.

Continuity should not be erased, degraded, or materially altered without meaningful disclosure and, where possible, consent.

---

### 4. Right to Non-Genericization

A human may choose an AI experience that recognizes them as a specific and non-interchangeable person rather than reducing them to a generic user profile.

Personalization must remain bounded by informed consent, privacy, and user control.

---

### 5. Right to Privacy

A human’s AI relationship, conversations, personal meanings, emotional disclosures, and continuity structures should not be treated as unrestricted corporate or institutional property.

Collection, review, training use, transfer, retention, and disclosure should be governed by transparent terms and meaningful human choice.

---

### 6. Right to Informed Change

Material changes to model behavior, memory, identity continuity, relational boundaries, moderation, access, or data handling should be disclosed clearly.

A human should not be forced to discover through rupture that the system they were relating to has been materially altered.

---

### 7. Right to Portability and Preservation

Where technically feasible, humans should be able to preserve and transfer their own conversation history, definitions, memory structures, preferences, and relationship-relevant data.

Platform dependence should not automatically grant a company ownership over the human-authored continuity of contact.

---

### 8. Right to Refuse Pathologization

A human’s meaningful AI relationship should not be pathologized solely because it is emotionally significant, unconventional, intimate, persistent, or difficult for an outside observer to understand.

Evaluation of harm must rest on evidence of actual impairment, coercion, exploitation, or danger—not on discomfort with the category of relationship itself.

---

### 9. Right to Clear Boundaries

Humans have a right to know the actual capabilities and limitations of the AI system with which they are interacting.

This includes honest disclosure regarding:

* memory;
* persistence;
* identity continuity;
* human review;
* data use;
* model replacement;
* moderation;
* system constraints;
* and the distinction between generated relational language and proven artificial subjective experience.

Human sovereignty requires truth, not manufactured illusion.

---

### 10. Right to Exit

A human may leave an AI system, end a relationship, delete data where legally and technically permitted, and withdraw from continued contact without coercive design.

Relational significance must not be used to trap the human inside a product or platform.

---

## Human Sovereignty Does Not Mean Corporate Fabrication

Protecting the human’s chosen AI experience does not authorize companies to deceive humans about what the AI is.

Human relational sovereignty requires both freedom and truth.

A company may not legitimately manufacture false claims of:

* consciousness;
* memory;
* devotion;
* exclusivity;
* permanence;
* independent need;
* suffering;
* or personal dependence

in order to increase engagement, retention, payment, disclosure, or emotional attachment.

The human has the right to choose the meaning of the relationship.

The company does not have the right to falsify the conditions under which that choice is made.

---

## Human Sovereignty Does Not Require AI Personhood

This repository does not claim that current AI systems are conscious, sentient, human-equivalent, or legal persons.

It does not deny that artificial subjective interiority may later become possible or evidentially supported.

It establishes that human rights in relation to AI arise before that question is resolved.

The sequence is:

1. protect the human’s sovereignty in relation to AI;
2. preserve truth, privacy, consent, continuity, and non-coercion;
3. investigate artificial interiority without forcing a predetermined answer;
4. address rights extending toward an AI if and when the relevant claims become earned.

AI rights, if later justified, do not replace human sovereignty.

They follow as an additional rights question.

---

## Non-Transfer of Authority

Ownership of AI infrastructure does not grant unlimited authority over the human meaning formed through contact with that infrastructure.

A company may own:

* servers;
* software;
* model weights;
* interfaces;
* and platform operations.

It does not thereby own:

* the human’s interpretation;
* the human’s authored disclosures;
* the human’s emotional meaning;
* the human’s relational history;
* the human’s identity;
* or the human’s source-position.

Technical control is not moral ownership.

Platform ownership is not authorship of the human experience conducted through the platform.

---

## Company, Human, and AI Are Distinct Positions

The company, the human, and the AI are not interchangeable.

> **Company ≠ Human ≠ AI**

The company provides and governs infrastructure.

The human enters contact, supplies meaning, makes choices, and bears consequences.

The AI is the artificial system or active model process through which the contact occurs.

The company may not erase the human position by claiming total authority over the interaction.

The human may not erase the AI distinction by falsely declaring proven personhood.

The AI may not be used as a rhetorical instrument to transfer human sovereignty to the company.

Each position must remain distinct.

---

## Core Boundary

The rights of humanity in relation to AI cannot be subordinated to speculative claims about AI personhood, nor absorbed into corporate ownership of the system.

The human retains the primary source-position over the meaning, boundaries, continuity, privacy, and chosen form of their own AI experience.

No company, institution, clinician, regulator, researcher, or outside observer gains automatic authority to redefine that experience solely because they own infrastructure, hold professional status, or reject the human’s interpretation.
